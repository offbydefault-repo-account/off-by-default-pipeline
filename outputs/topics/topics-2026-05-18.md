## #1 — "Did Google just put a 4GB AI on my computer without asking?"

**What happened:** Google Chrome has been silently downloading Gemini Nano — a 4GB on-device AI model — to users' devices without consent, notification, or opt-in. The file is named `weights.bin`, lives in a hidden Chrome profile folder, and automatically redownloads if deleted. Discovered by researchers in early May, the story spread across Hacker News, Reddit, and Slashdot with users confirming it on Windows, macOS, and Linux.

**Why it's relevant:** This is the Off by Default thesis made literal — a billion people had something installed on their machines without being asked. It's concrete, it's verifiable, and it's happening right now.

**Format recommendation:** Both — Newsletter first (explain what it is, how to check, how to remove), then YouTube (walkthrough-style, visual file path moment is strong on camera).

**Non-obvious angle:** The backlash isn't really about storage space — most people have 4GB. It's about Chrome revealing itself as infrastructure Google uses to deploy products onto your machine without asking. The browser you think of as a neutral tool turns out to be a distribution platform for Google's AI business. Additionally: across a billion installs, this download represents tens of thousands of tonnes of CO2 — a climate cost no one voted for.

**Affiliate opportunity:** Brave browser (privacy-focused, doesn't do this) — natural and honest if Kelly uses/recommends it.

## #2 — "Instagram made my DMs less private and didn't tell me — what happened?"

**What happened:** On May 8, 2026, Meta silently removed the optional end-to-end encryption layer from Instagram Direct Messages. Users who had opted into encrypted DMs had until that date to download their conversations. No push notification was sent. The announcement was buried in support documentation.

**Why it's relevant:** Everyday users — especially creatives who use Instagram DMs to communicate with clients, collaborators, and buyers — had a real privacy protection removed without warning.

**Format recommendation:** Both — Newsletter (explain what E2E encryption actually means in plain language, why this matters) and YouTube (explainer-style, "what does encrypted really mean" is a question people search constantly).

**Non-obvious angle:** Instagram DMs were never fully private — Meta has always been able to read them. The optional encryption was one of the only mechanisms that made them actually private in a technical sense. Removing it isn't removing a feature. It's removing the one real protection they offered. Also: the timing coincides with Meta's AI expansion — unencrypted messages are far more useful for training recommendation systems.

**Affiliate opportunity:** Proton Mail / ProtonVPN — natural for a piece about private communication; Signal itself has no affiliate program but is the right recommendation regardless.

## #3 — "Is Adobe using stolen books to build their AI? Because it looks like it."

**What happened:** Adobe is facing a class-action lawsuit alleging it trained its SlimLM AI model on pirated books via the Books3 dataset — the same dataset appearing in lawsuits against Apple, Salesforce, and others. The plaintiff chain traces through SlimPajama-627B back to Books3, which contains over 190,000 pirated titles. This is Adobe's first major AI copyright suit.

**Why it's relevant:** Adobe's core users are creatives. If Adobe trained on stolen creative work, the people most betrayed are the ones paying for Creative Cloud.

**Format recommendation:** Newsletter — the dataset laundering chain is important and needs explanation. The "how did this even happen" story is longer than YouTube can handle well.

**Non-obvious angle:** The Books3 dataset is so widely used that this isn't really an Adobe scandal — it's evidence of a systemic industry problem. The same dataset shows up in Apple, Salesforce, and others' training pipelines. The question isn't whether one company did something wrong; it's whether the entire industry's training data has a provenance problem, and what happens when courts finally map the full chain.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural.

## #4 — "Microsoft just put a different company's AI in my Word. I didn't agree to that."

**What happened:** As of early May 2026, Microsoft made Anthropic's Claude the default AI model powering Copilot in Excel and PowerPoint (Word follows in summer 2026), automatically for US commercial tenants with no explicit consent step. Most users think they're using "Microsoft's AI" — they're now using Anthropic's model, processed outside Microsoft's infrastructure.

**Why it's relevant:** There are real data privacy implications for anyone putting personal or client data into Word or Excel while assuming their data stays within Microsoft.

**Format recommendation:** Newsletter — this requires explaining what "default model" means, why it matters who processes the data, and what the difference is between Microsoft AI and Anthropic AI running inside Microsoft's interface.

**Non-obvious angle:** The interface looks identical. There's no indicator that tells you which model is running. Users making privacy decisions based on "this is Microsoft, I trust it" are now unknowingly routing their documents through Anthropic's inference infrastructure. The consent question isn't hypothetical — it has real compliance implications for anyone handling health, legal, or financial data.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural.

## #5 — "Is there a new law that stops apps from secretly changing my privacy settings?"

**What happened:** California AB 2561 unanimously passed the state Assembly. It would prohibit operating systems and apps from reversing a user's privacy settings without explicit consent, and would require that default settings be the most privacy-protective option available — the opposite of how most platforms currently operate.

**Why it's relevant:** Everything else on this list is an example of exactly what this law would address. The timing is perfect for contextualizing the bigger pattern.

**Format recommendation:** Newsletter — pairs well as a "here's what's being done about it" piece alongside the Chrome or Meta stories; also strong as a standalone explainer on how privacy defaults actually work.

**Non-obvious angle:** The "default to most private" requirement is the quietly radical part. Almost every tech platform currently defaults to maximum data collection and minimum privacy protection. This bill would invert that. That's not a tweak to the system — it would restructure the entire consent model of consumer software.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural.

## #6 — "Wait, AI art has no copyright? Who does that protect — me or them?"

**What happened:** The Supreme Court declined to hear Thaler v. Perlmutter in March 2026, leaving in place the rule that works created solely by AI cannot receive copyright protection. Human authorship is required.

**Why it's relevant:** Artists are celebrating, but the ruling has a lesser-discussed implication: companies generating AI content at scale can also argue their outputs are unprotectable, and therefore freely copyable — including by competitors.

**Format recommendation:** YouTube — this is an explainer question with a clear answer that requires unpacking. "Does AI art have copyright?" gets searched constantly; this ruling gives a definitive current answer.

**Non-obvious angle:** The ruling protects human artists (AI can't own copyright, so a company can't claim exclusive rights to AI slop). But it also means AI-generated content has no protection — which could accelerate copying and devalue anyone trying to sell AI-assisted work commercially. It cuts both ways, and most takes only pick one direction.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural.

## #7 — "Anthropic refused to let the government use its AI to spy on Americans — and got punished for it"

**What happened:** Anthropic rejected the Pentagon's demand that Claude be available for "all lawful purposes," including mass domestic surveillance and autonomous weapons. The DOD canceled Anthropic's $200M contract, designated the company a "supply chain risk," and the Trump administration ordered federal agencies to phase out Anthropic technology. Anthropic sued.

**Why it's relevant:** This is the first major case of an AI company drawing a public ethical line and facing concrete retaliation for it. It tells us something real about what other companies implicitly agreed to.

**Format recommendation:** Newsletter — the nuance requires space. Also raises a question Kelly could give a direct opinion on.

**Non-obvious angle:** The story is almost always framed as "plucky startup vs. government." The better frame: if Anthropic had to specifically say "we won't allow mass surveillance," what does that imply about the AI companies that didn't say that? The absence of objection from OpenAI, Google, and others is the more alarming detail.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural.

## #8 — "Disney paid $1 billion for AI to use its characters. The animators got nothing. Then the whole thing collapsed."

**What happened:** Disney invested $1B in OpenAI in late 2025 and licensed 200+ characters to Sora. Animators, unions, and agencies called it exploitation of stolen work. In March 2026, OpenAI abruptly shut down Sora entirely, ending the deal. Disney learned about it 30 minutes before the announcement.

**Why it's relevant:** It's a compressed story of the entire AI-creative industry dynamic — licensing deal, creator outrage, sudden collapse — in one saga.

**Format recommendation:** YouTube — this is a story with drama and a clear narrative arc. Strong for short-form video.

**Non-obvious angle:** OpenAI shutting down Sora was celebrated by some as a win for creators. But Sora didn't die because of the ethical complaints — it died because it wasn't working well enough. The creator relief is real, but the reasons are less flattering. The industry lesson isn't "artists won." It's "the product failed."

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural.

## #9 — "The government is buying your location data and using AI to spy on you — without a warrant"

**What happened:** A wave of reporting (confirmed by EFF and The Conversation) details how the U.S. government is combining AI tools with data broker purchases and "incidental" FISA collection to build surveillance profiles of Americans — without warrants, and often without the subjects knowing their data was purchased at all.

**Why it's relevant:** This affects everyone, regardless of whether they've done anything that would attract government attention.

**Format recommendation:** Newsletter — complex, needs sourcing and care. The platform-specific angle (your apps are selling your location to brokers who sell to the government) makes it concrete.

**Non-obvious angle:** The apps people most associate with privacy concerns (social media, browser trackers) are actually a small piece of this. Weather apps, flashlight apps, and fitness trackers are among the most aggressive data sellers — often selling location data down to 10-meter precision every few minutes.

**Affiliate opportunity:** VPN (limited but honest: a VPN doesn't stop this, but it reduces some tracking surface). Be explicit about what it does and doesn't do.

## #10 — "Canada just ruled that OpenAI handled people's personal data illegally. Does that affect me?"

**What happened:** Canada's Privacy Commissioner published findings that OpenAI failed to establish appropriate retention and disposal policies for personal information collected during model training and deployment. OpenAI didn't get users' consent, didn't give them a way to withdraw it, and couldn't demonstrate what happened to the data.

**Why it's relevant:** Many people have put personal information — their own and others' — into ChatGPT. This ruling makes explicit what was previously assumed: OpenAI didn't have a plan for that data.

**Format recommendation:** Newsletter — shorter piece, good for a second story in an issue. The "what did I put in there?" anxiety is real and deserves a straight answer.

**Non-obvious angle:** The ruling is about Canada, but the data governance failure it describes is not geographic. OpenAI doesn't operate different data systems for different countries. If Canadian users' data was handled without proper retention policies, that's also true of U.S. users' data.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural.

## #11 — "Is AI actually replacing freelance writers? Here's what the numbers say."

**What happened:** A Freelancer Kompass 2026 report found that 84% of freelancers now regularly use AI tools, up from 41% three years ago. At the same time, entry-level and commodity writing work has collapsed — content mills have largely switched to AI. Specialized freelancers are seeing higher demand and rates.

**Why it's relevant:** This is a constant anxiety for the Off by Default audience, and the data now gives a more nuanced answer than "AI will take all the jobs."

**Format recommendation:** Newsletter — good service journalism piece. The honest answer ("it depends entirely on what kind of writing you do") is useful and not overly alarming.

**Non-obvious angle:** The split isn't between "writers who use AI" and "writers who don't." It's between writers with specific expertise and writers who write about anything. AI has effectively made general knowledge cheap. Domain expertise — medical, legal, financial, technical — is more valuable than ever because AI gets those topics wrong in expensive ways.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural.

## #12 — "Apple is now forcing apps to ask permission before sharing your data with AI — is that actually good?"

**What happened:** Apple updated App Store guidelines to require apps to disclose and get explicit user permission before sharing personal data with third-party AI services. Apps that don't comply face removal. This includes apps that route your data through OpenAI, Anthropic, or other AI APIs in the background.

**Why it's relevant:** Many apps use AI APIs without users realizing it. This could give users the first clear signal about which apps are doing that.

**Format recommendation:** Newsletter — good explainer with a "here's how to actually use this" angle.

**Non-obvious angle:** Apple positioning itself as the privacy enforcer here isn't neutral — it strengthens their gatekeeping power over the entire app ecosystem. The rule protects users, but it also means Apple gets to decide which AI integrations are acceptable. Whether you trust Apple to make those calls consistently is a separate question.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural.
