## #1 — "Did Google secretly put AI on my computer without asking?"
**What happened:** Google Chrome has been silently downloading a 4GB file called `weights.bin` to user devices through routine browser updates. The file contains Gemini Nano, Google's on-device AI model. If you find and delete it, Chrome downloads it again. There was no opt-in, no pop-up, no notification.

**Why this audience cares:** Two out of three internet users use Chrome. Every single one of them has this file on their computer and doesn't know it. That's a concrete violation of the feeling that your computer is yours.

**Format:** Both — Newsletter for the "how to check, what to do" angle; YouTube for a visual walkthrough of finding the file.

**Non-obvious angle:** The file lives in a folder called `OptGuideOnDeviceModel` — a deliberately bureaucratic name chosen to be unfindable. More importantly: the model isn't just for Chrome features. It powers a "Summarizer API" that *any website* can call. Users are hosting AI infrastructure for the entire web without knowing it.

**Why this audience cares:** Creators, writers, and artists use Instagram DMs for real conversations — pitches, collabs, sensitive exchanges. Many assumed the platform was more private than it is.

**Format:** Both — Newsletter for the stakes and alternatives; YouTube for the plain-language explanation of what encryption actually means.

**Non-obvious angle:** Meta's "low adoption" argument is structurally circular. They made the setting invisible, required opt-in per conversation, never sent a notification it existed, then cited the resulting low numbers as evidence nobody wanted it. The data doesn't prove user indifference. It proves Meta's design choices.

## #6 — "The Supreme Court ruled AI can't own copyright — but what does that mean for *my* AI-assisted work?"
**What happened:** In March 2026, the Supreme Court declined to hear Thaler v. Perlmutter, leaving in place the ruling that AI cannot be a copyright author. Works created autonomously by AI without human creative input cannot be copyrighted.

**Why this audience cares:** Anyone who uses AI tools in creative work — writing, illustration, music — needs to understand what's protected and what isn't.

**Format:** Newsletter — practical breakdown of the ruling's real-world implications for creators.

**Non-obvious angle:** The case was decided on extreme facts — Thaler explicitly disclaimed *any* human input. The ruling doesn't resolve the much more common situation (human uses AI as a tool). That ambiguity is still completely open, and platforms are writing their own rules to fill it.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None.
