## #1 — Meta Muse Image: Instagram AI Photos Without Consent

**Worried person's search query:** "Can anyone use my Instagram photos to make AI images of me without asking?"

**Why it's relevant:** Meta launched a feature on July 7 that let any user tag a public Instagram account and generate AI images using that account's photos as reference — no consent required. SAG-AFTRA urged members and all users to opt out. Meta pulled the Instagram integration on July 13 after six days of backlash — but Muse Image still runs in the Meta AI app and WhatsApp, and the opt-out framework that existed remains in place.

**Format:** Both — Newsletter and YouTube. The story has a clear narrative arc (launch → backlash → partial rollback), an obvious villain, and a concrete action step, which works in both formats.

**Non-obvious angle:** The rollback looked like a win, but it wasn't a change in principle — it was a change in visibility. What got pulled was the Instagram-specific tagging feature, not the underlying logic that public content is reusable as AI reference material. The opt-out is still opt-out. That's worth dwelling on.

**Affiliate opportunity:** Privacy-focused Instagram alternative (Glass for photographers, Pixelfed for general use) — only if Kelly has personal experience with one.

## #2 — Google AI Training on Your Lens Photos and Voice Searches

**Worried person's search query:** "Is Google using my photos and voice notes to train its AI without telling me?"

**Why it's relevant:** In June 2026, Google quietly updated its Search Services privacy settings so that photos sent through Lens, voice queries, files uploaded to Translate, and similar search inputs are captured for AI training by default. TechCrunch flagged it July 6. The opt-out exists but requires knowing where to look. Most people received an email about it and closed it without reading.

**Format:** Both — Newsletter and YouTube. Very practical, clear action step, huge audience (anyone who uses Google Search tools), and a non-obvious distinction that's perfect for explanation content.

**Non-obvious angle:** This is not about Google Photos, which is explicitly excluded — and that distinction is exactly what makes it confusing. The photos affected are the ones you used as live search tools: the rash you Lens'd to check, the menu you photographed to translate, the receipt you uploaded. Task inputs, not memories. That reframe lands.

**Affiliate opportunity:** Alternative search engine with no-logging policy (DuckDuckGo, Kagi) — only if Kelly uses one and has an affiliate relationship.

## #3 — Microsoft 365 Copilot Is Auto-Installing on Work Computers

**Worried person's search query:** "Why did Microsoft Copilot suddenly appear on my work computer and how do I get rid of it?"

**Why it's relevant:** Between mid-June and mid-July 2026, Microsoft began automatically installing the Microsoft 365 Copilot app on eligible Windows 11 devices with commercial M365 subscriptions. No individual user opt-out exists — only IT administrators can block it, via a Group Policy setting that had to be applied before the rollout reached devices. European Economic Area tenants are excluded; US users are not.

**Format:** Newsletter. The audience of everyday workers using M365 is massive; the "my company installed something on my computer without asking me" story maps directly to Off by Default's premise.

**Non-obvious angle:** The user has no individual recourse here. This is the first prominent example of an AI feature deployment where the opt-out belongs entirely to the employer's IT department, not the person whose computer it is. That asymmetry is the real story.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural.

## #4 — Your Fitness Tracker Is a Privacy Risk — EFF Report

**Worried person's search query:** "Is my smartwatch sharing my health data with the government or advertisers?"

**Why it's relevant:** An EFF investigation published July 19 found that of ten major wearable brands, only Apple encrypts health data end-to-end. Fitness tracker data — heart rate, GPS location, sleep records — falls outside HIPAA, meaning a government subpoena unlocks what most companies store as fully readable data. Surveillance firm Penlink already markets fitness and wearable data to ICE and other law enforcement agencies as "an overlooked investigative resource."

**Format:** YouTube. This is a visceral, surprising story with a concrete antagonist (Penlink) and a clear takeaway. The visual medium lets you show the categories of data at stake.

**Non-obvious angle:** Health data that falls outside HIPAA but sits inside your Oura ring is not protected medical information. Your wearable knows your movement patterns, your sleep, your resting heart rate — and none of that has the legal protection people assume it does when they think "health data." The gap between how private it feels and how private it legally is is the story.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural — don't recommend a "safer" wearable without full research.

## #5 — The Opt-Out Architecture: Every Major Platform Now Defaults AI On

**Worried person's search query:** "Why do I have to keep turning off AI features I didn't ask for?"

**Why it's relevant:** This week's Meta and Google stories are part of a consistent pattern: Google, Apple, Meta, and Microsoft all now ship AI features switched on by default. Apple Intelligence rewrites notifications and summarizes emails on every eligible iPhone without asking. Meta AI occupies the search bar across its entire app family. A piece published this week called July 1, 2026 "the day opt-in died."

**Format:** Newsletter. A meta-framing issue that explains the week's pattern and gives readers a concept to carry forward — "off by default" vs. "on by default."

**Non-obvious angle:** The opt-out is not an oversight. It's a business model. Every user who doesn't opt out generates training data, usage signals, and engagement metrics. The architecture of modern AI rollouts is specifically designed to make the training-data-generating behavior the path of least resistance.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural.

## #6 — Adobe Is Quietly Adding AI Content to Your Files

**Worried person's search query:** "Did Adobe add AI-generated content to my files without telling me?"

**Why it's relevant:** Adobe's PDF export tool has been generating AI alt text automatically — and overwriting existing alt text — in users' files without warning. Evil Hat Productions went public about discovering this in their Umdaar game PDFs. Adobe has also raised Creative Cloud All Apps prices from $59.99 to $69.99/month while introducing AI features by default, prompting creators to cancel.

**Format:** Newsletter. Core audience overlap with creatives using Adobe tools daily. The "something changed in my file" story is visceral and concrete.

**Non-obvious angle:** The alt text issue isn't just annoying — it's an accessibility problem. Manually written alt text for images in documents exists to help screen reader users. AI-generated replacement alt text is often generic and less useful. Adobe's auto-injection can degrade accessibility while presenting itself as an improvement.

**Affiliate opportunity:** Affinity Publisher or Canva (if used) as Adobe alternatives.

## #7 — The KIDS Act Would Protect Kids by Surveilling Everyone

**Worried person's search query:** "Is the online child safety bill actually going to track all adults online?"

**Why it's relevant:** The House passed the KIDS Act, requiring platforms to implement age verification and age-gating. The EFF and digital rights groups oppose it, arguing it will mandate surveillance of all users to identify which ones are minors — meaning every adult would need to prove their age to use platforms that might have minors on them.

**Format:** Newsletter. Classic "the solution is worse than the problem" angle. Relevant to any creator with an online audience.

**Non-obvious angle:** Age verification at scale requires ID verification at scale. That means every platform handling the law's requirements would need to collect and store identity documents — or outsource that to third-party identity brokers. The child safety bill could become the largest single-event expansion of surveillance infrastructure since the Patriot Act.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural.

## #8 — The Government Is Buying Your Data to Skip the Warrant

**Worried person's search query:** "Can the government use AI to spy on me without a warrant by just buying my data?"

**Why it's relevant:** The Supreme Court ruled 6-3 in late June that people have a reasonable expectation of privacy for cellphone location data (Chatrie v. U.S.) — but a separate loophole remains: the government can purchase data from data brokers, including AI-processed aggregations of movement, behavior, and location, without a warrant. The Anthropic-Pentagon dispute earlier this year brought this to mainstream attention.

**Format:** Newsletter. Slow-burn issue that connects many dots readers haven't connected.

**Non-obvious angle:** The Supreme Court's location data ruling is being reported as a privacy win — and in a narrow sense, it is. But it didn't touch the data broker market. The government can't demand your phone location without a warrant, but it can buy the same information from a broker who compiled it from apps on your phone. The ruling doesn't close that gap.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural.

## #9 — AI Copyright 2026: What Creators Can Actually Protect

**Worried person's search query:** "Can I copyright my AI-assisted artwork or writing — what does the law actually say now?"

**Why it's relevant:** The Supreme Court declined to hear Thaler v. Perlmutter in March 2026, leaving in place the human authorship requirement for copyright. AI-assisted works can be copyrighted, but only when a human exercises meaningful creative control — entering prompts alone is not enough. Ongoing cases against Anthropic, Meta, OpenAI, and Midjourney continue to define whether training on copyrighted work constitutes fair use.

**Format:** Newsletter. Evergreen concern for the core creative audience. The legal landscape is finally concrete enough to summarize clearly.

**Non-obvious angle:** The ruling that AI can't copyright its own work is being celebrated by creators — but the flip side is that AI companies using copyrighted work to train their models are still in the middle of years-long fair use litigation. The thing that protects creators (human authorship) and the thing that threatens them (training data use) are two separate legal questions. Most coverage conflates them.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural — potentially mention a lawyer-verified resource for creators.

## #10 — Penlink: The Company Selling Your Wearable Data to ICE

**Worried person's search query:** "Who is buying health data from fitness trackers and what are they doing with it?"

**Why it's relevant:** Surveillance firm Penlink markets fitness trackers and wearables as investigative tools to law enforcement clients including ICE, specifically because wearables establish movement patterns and heart rate changes — data that sits outside HIPAA and is stored as fully readable text by most wearable companies. This is a specific actor, not an abstract threat.

**Format:** YouTube. A named company, documented marketing materials, and a concrete mechanism make this highly credible and shareable.

**Non-obvious angle:** Penlink doesn't need a data breach or a hack. They buy data that's already available or obtain it through legal process because the companies storing it never encrypted it. The threat model here isn't a hacker — it's a legal subpoena to a company that made the business decision to store your heart rate data in plain text.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural.

## #11 — Surveillance Pricing: New Jersey Bans Grocery Stores From Charging You More Based on Your Data

**Worried person's search query:** "Are grocery stores charging me different prices based on my data?"

**Why it's relevant:** New Jersey's legislature passed the Fair Price Protection Act, banning surveillance pricing for groceries — making it the first US state to explicitly ban algorithmic pricing that charges different customers different amounts based on data profiles. The practice already exists in retail, travel, and food delivery.

**Format:** Newsletter. Short, punchy, concrete — works as a sidebar or a short issue. Also a useful "things are fighting back" counterpoint to heavier surveillance stories.

**Non-obvious angle:** Grocery surveillance pricing hits lower-income shoppers hardest, because the data profiles of people who clip coupons, shop late, or live in certain zip codes lead to higher algorithmic prices, not lower ones. The "personalized pricing" framing makes it sound like a benefit when the mechanism works against the people it's presented as helping.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural.

## #12 — Google Ads Auto-Authorized AI to Manage Your Campaigns

**Worried person's search query:** "Did Google just give itself permission to run my ads automatically with AI?"

**Why it's relevant:** Google's July 1 Terms of Service update bound every Google Ads account to revised terms that authorize Google to use automated features to format, select, or generate targets, ads, or destinations on the advertiser's behalf — as a default authorization, with no login prompt, checkbox, or opt-in step. Small business owners and creator-entrepreneurs running their own ads are affected.

**Format:** Newsletter. Niche but deeply relevant to the subset of the audience running small ad budgets.

**Non-obvious angle:** This is not just an AI feature rollout — it's a change in what Google's contract with advertisers means. The revised terms shift the default responsibility for ad decisions toward Google and away from the advertiser. For small spenders who aren't monitoring campaigns daily, this could mean budget being spent in ways they didn't authorize.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural.
