### #1 — "Is Meta using my private chats with its AI to target ads at me?"

**Why it's relevant:** Meta started routing conversations with Meta AI (on Facebook, Instagram, and WhatsApp) into its advertising system in December 2025. There is no opt-out for US users. Most users have no idea this is happening.

**Format:** Both — Newsletter primary, YouTube companion
The newsletter lets readers sit with the opt-out gap between the US and EU. The YouTube hook writes itself: "your private chats are being used to sell you things."

**Non-obvious angle:** The EU, UK, and South Korea all have opt-outs because regulation forced Meta to build them. The US doesn't — not because the technology doesn't exist, but because no one required it. The opt-out already exists. US users just weren't given it.

**Affiliate opportunity:** Proton (proton.me) — privacy-focused email/messaging; fits naturally as the "what you can switch to" recommendation.

### #2 — "Apple said Siri protects my privacy. Why is Google now processing what I ask it?"

**Why it's relevant:** WWDC 2026 (June 8) revealed that the new Siri AI hands off complex queries to Google Gemini running on Google Cloud servers. Apple's core brand promise is privacy. This creates a direct contradiction that iPhone users — especially privacy-motivated ones — will want explained.

**Format:** Both — YouTube primary (very visual, fast-moving), Newsletter companion
The Siri/Gemini story has high visual potential (three-tier architecture diagram) and fits the anxious-iPhone-user's search query format perfectly.

**Non-obvious angle:** Apple didn't make this privacy decision — they made a business decision (Gemini is years ahead of what Apple could build in-house) and retrofitted the privacy language around it. "Privacy in AI is nonnegotiable" was said by the same person who just handed Siri to Google.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None forced. Possibly Proton if the piece gets into alternatives, but don't stretch it.

### #3 — "Google just gave website owners a way to block AI from using their content — but why can't I do that as a reader?"

**Why it's relevant:** As of June 3, 2026, publishers can opt out of having their content scraped for Google AI Overviews. Regular users still can't opt out of having content summarized instead of seeing the original source. The gap between who gets control and who doesn't is the story.

**Format:** Newsletter
The audience of writers and creatives will recognize the asymmetry immediately — platforms give control to entities with legal leverage, not to individuals.

**Non-obvious angle:** The UK's CMA forced this — it was the first binding AI opt-out regulation globally. Without that regulatory pressure, publishers still wouldn't have the toggle. The question it raises: who advocates for individual users the way the CMA advocated for publishers?

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural.

### #4 — "The authors who sued Anthropic are getting paid. But some of them walked away from the settlement. Why?"

**Why it's relevant:** The $1.5B Bartz v. Anthropic settlement has payment distributions beginning June 2026. Simultaneously, authors who opted out filed a new lawsuit against Anthropic in California federal court on June 1. For self-publishers and working writers, this is the copyright AI story that's actually moving.

**Format:** Newsletter
Dense with nuance. The two-track development (settlement payments + new lawsuit) is a story that needs editorial guidance more than a quick YouTube hook.

**Non-obvious angle:** Authors who accepted the settlement can't sue again. Authors who opted out are starting from scratch. The settlement may have split the author community into those who took the money and those who believe a bigger principle is at stake — and both are rational choices given different circumstances.

**Affiliate opportunity:** The Authors Guild membership — natural fit for writer-audience.

### #5 — "YouTube now wants a biometric video of my face to prove I'm a real creator. Is that normal?"

**Why it's relevant:** YouTube's 2026 platform updates require identity verification via government-issued ID and a biometric reference video to manage deepfakes and impersonation at scale. Creators who want full monetization access are being asked to give the platform their face data.

**Format:** YouTube (ironic), then Newsletter
The YouTube format is the right place to discuss YouTube's policies with creators. The biometric angle is new territory that hasn't been widely explained to non-technical creators.

**Non-obvious angle:** Once you submit a biometric reference video to YouTube, you've created a template that Google now holds indefinitely. The policy exists to fight impersonation — a real problem — but the solution gives Google something it didn't previously have: a verified facial reference for millions of real people.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural.

### #6 — "AI can't be a copyright author — the Supreme Court confirmed it. But what does that actually mean for me?"

**Why it's relevant:** In March 2026 the Supreme Court declined to hear Thaler v. Perlmutter, letting stand the ruling that AI-generated work without human authorship cannot be copyrighted. This is clarifying — but the "human contribution" threshold is still undefined, which matters for creatives using AI as a tool.

**Format:** Newsletter
Requires nuanced explanation. The "good news" framing is too easy — the non-obvious angle is that the ruling opens questions about how much human involvement is enough.

**Non-obvious angle:** The ruling doesn't say AI-assisted work can't be copyrighted — it says AI-only work can't. That means the next legal battleground is: what counts as "enough" human authorship when you're using AI as a creative tool? That question is completely open.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural.

### #7 — "Meta replaced human content moderators with AI. What does that mean when I try to appeal something?"

**Why it's relevant:** Meta has significantly reduced human moderation staff and expanded AI systems to handle content decisions. For creators and everyday users who've ever tried to appeal a wrongful takedown or account restriction, this changes who — or what — is reviewing your case.

**Format:** Newsletter
The "my post got wrongly taken down and now I'm talking to a robot" experience is deeply familiar to the target audience. This adds a layer of specificity to that frustration.

**Non-obvious angle:** Human moderators were inconsistent and often wrong — but they were also accountable, identifiable, and capable of judgment in edge cases. AI moderation is consistent in a different way: it's consistently unable to understand context. The question is whether "faster and more consistent" is actually better when the thing being moderated is nuanced human expression.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural.

Nobody knows where the line is, which makes every creator who uses any AI tool slightly nervous about every upload.

### #9 — "Colorado just passed America's first real AI law. Does it protect me, or just companies?"

**Why it's relevant:** The Colorado AI Act takes effect June 30, 2026 — the first comprehensive US state law requiring impact assessments and consumer disclosures for "high-risk" AI systems. This is the first time American users have received any legal baseline protection from AI decision-making.

**Format:** Newsletter
Policy-heavy but important for the "someone is finally doing something" narrative this audience needs. Needs framing that makes it concrete and personal.

**Non-obvious angle:** "High-risk AI" under the Colorado law means AI making decisions about employment, housing, healthcare, and credit — not the AI in your chatbot or your email. Most everyday users won't be covered by this law for most of their AI interactions. It's a start, not a solution.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural.

### #10 — "Meta's own Instagram account recovery tool was used to hack 20,000+ accounts. What happened?"

**Why it's relevant:** Instagram's official account recovery tool was exploited by hackers who used it to take control of more than 20,000 accounts in the first week of June 2026. For anyone who's ever locked themselves out of Instagram and feared they couldn't get back in, this is both reassuring and terrifying.

**Format:** Newsletter (short), or a YouTube short
Concrete, personal, actionable — the "what to do right now" format.

**Non-obvious angle:** Recovery tools are a fundamental tension in security: the easier you make it to recover an account, the easier you make it to steal one. Meta made account recovery easier. Hackers found it first. This isn't a flaw Meta overlooked — it's a design tradeoff that went wrong, which is different.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural; could mention 2FA/passkey tools as protective steps.

### #11 — "SFWA and Comic-Con are banning AI work from contests. What actually counts as AI-generated?"

**Why it's relevant:** The Science Fiction and Fantasy Writers Association (SFWA) and San Diego Comic-Con both have established policies banning AI-generated work from awards and exhibitions. But "AI-generated" is not defined consistently, leaving creatives who use any AI tools in murky territory.

**Format:** Newsletter
This speaks directly to the creative community who are caught between using useful tools and being categorized as cheating.

**Non-obvious angle:** Most of these bans define "AI-generated" by outcome, not process. But the line between "AI-assisted" and "AI-generated" is philosophically undefined. A photo edited in Photoshop's AI tools — is that AI-generated? A story where AI wrote a rough draft that was heavily rewritten? Nobody agrees, and the policies aren't clarifying it.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None natural.

### #12 — "Researchers who didn't want to be studied are appearing in AI training data anyway — this week's privacy roundup"

**Why it's relevant:** Data protection authorities from 61 countries issued a joint statement earlier this year about AI systems generating realistic depictions of real people without consent. The Instagram hack, the Meta keystroke-tracking for employees, and ongoing data breach coverage all feed the same underlying anxiety: who controls data about me?

**Format:** Newsletter (weekly roundup sidebar)
Good "the bigger pattern" piece to contextualize multiple smaller stories.

**Non-obvious angle:** The data protection authorities' statement specifically highlights that consent models designed for the pre-AI web don't translate. You consented to have your photo on a platform. You did not consent to have it used to train a generation engine. Those are different things that current law treats the same.

**Affiliate opportunity:** Proton or similar privacy-first tools.
