## #1 — "Why did Microsoft install a new AI app on my work computer without asking me?"

**Why it's relevant:** Microsoft is mid-rollout right now — June 15 through July 15, 2026 — force-installing its Copilot app on every Windows machine running Microsoft 365 Business or Enterprise. It's the third time they've tried this after pulling back from backlash twice. Millions of Windows users are finding the icon on their taskbar this week without having installed anything.

**Format:** Both (Newsletter + YouTube). This is a "what just happened to my computer" story that works as a search-driven YouTube video and as an explainer newsletter.

**Non-obvious angle:** Microsoft isn't technically doing anything wrong — it's within its terms. But the company has decided that the *default state* is "Copilot is there," and any organization or person who doesn't want it has the obligation to remove it. They've inverted consent: opt-out is the work, not opt-in. The EU exemption (EEA users don't get the auto-install by regulation) makes this legible — Microsoft knows what an acceptable default looks like. They just chose differently for everyone else.

**Affiliate opportunity:** LibreOffice / OnlyOffice as Microsoft 365 alternatives for the fully fed-up.

## #2 — "Did Google turn on AI in my Gmail without asking me?"

**Why it's relevant:** Google's Gemini AI features are on by default in Gmail for US users — summarizing emails, suggesting replies, training on inbox data. Users were auto-enrolled. A class-action lawsuit is now moving forward. The EU, UK, and Japan get these features turned off by default because local law requires opt-in. US users don't get the same protection.

**Format:** Both (Newsletter + YouTube). This is the "I want to know what Google is doing in my inbox" search that gets typed a lot.

**Non-obvious angle:** To fully opt out of Gemini's data use in Gmail, you have to navigate three different settings locations — and doing so also disables useful features like Smart Reply. Google has bundled AI training permission with AI productivity features, so opting out of one costs you the other. That's not a coincidence.

**Affiliate opportunity:** Proton Mail — free tier, end-to-end encrypted, no AI scanning.

## #3 — "Can the UK really force AI companies to pay artists for training data?"

**Why it's relevant:** The UK government proposed a plan that would let AI companies train on any creative work by default, with an opt-out for artists. After massive backlash from Elton John, Dua Lipa, Thom Yorke, and thousands of other artists, the government reversed course in March 2026 — but has no replacement policy. The outcome is legal limbo: artists won the fight but haven't won the war.

**Format:** Newsletter. This is a nuanced policy story that needs the space a newsletter affords.

**Non-obvious angle:** The opt-out model was almost as bad as no protection at all — it would have required every individual creator to flag their work in a machine-readable format to get protection, putting the burden entirely on the people with the least technical resources. The "win" here is avoiding that system, not actually establishing artist rights.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None.

## #4 — "Adobe killed the program I use to make animations. What am I supposed to use now?"

**Why it's relevant:** Adobe discontinued Animate in March 2026 after 25 years, citing resource reallocation to AI tools (Firefly). Animators, educators, and indie game developers who relied on it have no direct replacement — Adobe suggested After Effects and Express cover "portions" of the functionality. The backlash included calls to open-source the software.

**Format:** Newsletter + YouTube (short). A strong "how AI is changing what creative tools exist" story for the exact audience of both properties.

**Non-obvious angle:** Adobe isn't replacing Animate with something you can use — it's replacing it with something it can sell. The gap between "we're pivoting to AI features" and "here's what you use Monday morning" is where most everyday creatives actually live, and no one is talking about that gap.

**Affiliate opportunity:** Rough Animator, Moho, or Toon Boom Harmony as Animate alternatives.

## #5 — "The largest copyright settlement in US history was about AI training. Here's what authors actually got."

**Why it's relevant:** Bartz v. Anthropic settled for $1.5 billion — the largest publicly confirmed copyright payout in US history. It covers ~482,000 books at roughly $3,113 per title. Anthropic must destroy pirated dataset files. Claims deadline was March 30, 2026; final approval hearing was May 14. Many authors who should have claimed may have missed the window.

**Format:** Newsletter. A financial and legal literacy story for the creative audience.

**Non-obvious angle:** The settlement proves that the piracy of training data — not just the training itself — was the liability. A separate ruling (Judge Alsup's June 2025 opinion) found that training on copyrighted books is fair use, but that storing pirated copies is not. So AI companies may be legally fine to train on your work if they got it legitimately — even without asking you.

**Affiliate opportunity:** Authors Guild membership (they led this fight).

## #6 — "Meta is using my conversations with its AI to show me ads. Did I agree to this?"

**Why it's relevant:** Meta updated its privacy policy in December 2025 to state that interactions with Meta AI — conversations, questions, recommendations — generate advertising signals that feed into its ad delivery system. Limited opt-out options. Class-action suits are forming. The reaction from users who expected their conversations to be private has been significant.

**Format:** Newsletter. Works as a deeper explainer of what Meta's AI privacy policy actually says vs. what users expected.

**Non-obvious angle:** The line Meta is drawing is that person-to-person DMs are not collected — only conversations *with Meta AI*. The problem is that many users don't clearly distinguish between messaging a friend and asking Meta's AI assistant a question. The interface looks similar enough that the privacy difference is easy to miss.

**Affiliate opportunity:** Signal or Telegram as alternatives for private communication.

## #7 — "TikTok is automatically labeling AI videos now — what counts, and what happens if you don't comply?"

**Why it's relevant:** TikTok's 2026 policy requires explicit disclosure of AI-generated content across all post types, with automatic scanning for C2PA metadata. Non-compliance leads to removal, reduced distribution, or account suspension. Creators using AI tools to make videos are now operating in a murky compliance environment.

**Format:** YouTube. Short, action-oriented — who has to do what and what the stakes are.

**Non-obvious angle:** TikTok's scanner reads C2PA metadata embedded by tools like Adobe, Midjourney, and DALL-E — even if you didn't check the "disclose AI" box. So creators who used AI tools may be getting labeled without knowing it, and creators who manipulated AI content without thinking of it as "AI content" may be flagged when they expect not to be.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None.

## #8 — "The US government is using AI and data brokers to watch people — through your apps"

**Why it's relevant:** The Conversation and CDT have documented that US federal agencies are expanding mass surveillance capabilities through partnerships with commercial tech companies, data brokers, and apps. The current administration's June 2026 executive order on AI innovation has national security provisions that privacy advocates say could accelerate this.

**Format:** Newsletter. Requires context and nuance; a YouTube video risks being too alarmist without the space to be specific.

**Non-obvious angle:** The surveillance happening through apps isn't from hacking — it's from purchasing. Agencies buy data from brokers who bought it from apps. The apps are the ones your phone uses every day. This is technically legal. That's the part that makes it harder to fix.

## #9 — "Colorado had the first real AI consumer protection law. It got repealed before it ever started."

**Why it's relevant:** Colorado's AI Act (SB 24-205) was set to take effect June 30, 2026. On May 14, Governor Polis signed a new law (SB 189) that repeals and replaces it — weeks before implementation. The original law would have required impact assessments, consumer disclosures, and a right to appeal AI-driven decisions. The replacement takes effect January 2027 and emphasizes disclosure over consumer rights.

**Format:** Newsletter. Policy-nerd territory but has a clear everyday-reader angle: the law that was about to protect you got quietly replaced.

**Non-obvious angle:** The original law was replaced, in part, because tech companies lobbied hard against it — and the replacement is lighter on enforcement. The story being told publicly is "streamlining for business"; the story underneath it is "reducing accountability."

**Affiliate opportunity:** None.

## #10 — "What does Apple actually do with your data when you turn on Apple Intelligence?"

**Why it's relevant:** Apple's AI features (Apple Intelligence) are opt-in, unlike most competitors. But the App Store is now requiring third-party apps to get explicit permission before sharing user data with external AI services. This raises the question: if Apple's own AI requires opt-in, why are third-party apps only just now being required to ask?

**Format:** Newsletter or YouTube. A useful "here's what the privacy marketing actually means in practice" explainer for Apple users.

**Non-obvious angle:** Apple being "privacy-first" on AI is partly genuine architecture (on-device processing for many tasks) and partly marketing positioning against Google and Microsoft. The reality is that Private Cloud Compute — Apple's system for tasks that can't run on-device — still sends data to Apple's servers, with protections Apple has designed and Apple controls. "Trusting Apple's privacy model" is still trusting Apple.

**Affiliate opportunity:** None.

## #11 — "EU AI transparency rules kick in August 2026 — what actually changes for people who aren't in Europe?"

**Why it's relevant:** The EU AI Act's transparency obligations take effect August 2026, requiring AI systems to disclose when users are interacting with AI and ensuring high-risk AI outputs are labeled. This typically pushes US companies to update their global products, not just their EU versions — so these rules often end up improving disclosures everywhere.

**Format:** Newsletter. Requires context about how EU law affects global products.

**Non-obvious angle:** The EU isn't just regulating European users — it's effectively setting standards for what US companies do globally, because it's more efficient to build one product than two. The rules that protect European users this August will likely be what protects American users eventually, if at all. US users are currently benefiting from EU law without the EU vote.

## #12 — "What can't AI actually do for writers — and why isn't anyone being honest about it?"

**Why it's relevant:** Creatives are being told that AI can replace significant parts of their workflow, and many are feeling anxiety about what that means. The actual limitations of AI writing tools — no lived experience, no genuine originality, no emotional stakes — are underreported relative to the capabilities claims.

**Format:** Both. An "honest conversation" piece that serves the skeptical reader and the curious one equally.

**Non-obvious angle:** The limitation that matters most isn't that AI "can't feel" — it's that AI writing is trained on the past and can only produce well-weighted combinations of what already exists. When everyone uses the same tool to produce the same kinds of content, the result isn't more writing — it's more similar writing. The risk to writers isn't replacement; it's market saturation with mediocre AI output that makes human work harder to find.
