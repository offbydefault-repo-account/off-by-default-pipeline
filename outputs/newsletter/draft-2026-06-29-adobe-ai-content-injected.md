---

The complaint showed up first in a game designer's Bluesky post. The publisher, Evil Hat Productions, had updated the PDF files for one of their tabletop roleplaying games. Players downloading the new version noticed something unexpected: descriptive text had appeared in the file that Evil Hat hadn't written. Adobe's AI generated it automatically during a save operation. No prompt. No warning. No checkbox.

The detail that made the post spread: the AI text didn't just appear in empty fields where nothing existed before. It overwrote descriptions that were already there — text written by a human, for a specific purpose, that the file had contained before the update. Adobe's system replaced it with AI-generated alternatives. Without asking.

If you use Adobe Acrobat to work with PDFs, there's a reasonable chance this has already happened to your files too.

---

Alt text is the written description attached to images in digital documents. When someone uses a screen reader — software that reads documents aloud for people who are visually impaired — it reads the alt text to describe images the user can't see. When alt text is written carefully by a person who understands what an image means in context, it's one of the things that makes a document actually accessible. When it's generated by AI, it's a description of what the image looks like, without any understanding of what it means.

Adobe Acrobat has begun automatically generating AI alt text for images in documents when its AI features are active. The framing in Adobe's materials is that this improves accessibility — and generating alt text for images that genuinely have none is a real contribution. There are a lot of PDFs in the world with no alt text at all, and that's a genuine accessibility problem.

But that's not what happened here. Acrobat is also overwriting alt text that humans already wrote. Intentional descriptions, added by someone who cared about accessibility, replaced with AI guesses.
