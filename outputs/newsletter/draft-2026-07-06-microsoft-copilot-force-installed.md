## DRAFT BEGINS

A few months ago, Microsoft tried to automatically install its AI assistant on millions of Windows computers. Users noticed. Tech journalists wrote about it. Microsoft paused the rollout and cited a "technical issue." A lot of people exhaled and moved on.

It was not a retreat. The rollout resumed in June, and if you use Windows 11 with a Microsoft 365 subscription, the Copilot app is either already on your machine or it's arriving before the end of July.

This is not Microsoft offering you a new tool. This is Microsoft deciding the tool is installed.

---

Here's what Copilot actually is: a sidebar built into your Windows computer that can summarize documents, draft emails, answer questions, and pull from your Microsoft 365 data — your Outlook inbox, your Word files, your OneDrive. If you've used a chatbot like ChatGPT, the interaction is similar. The difference is that it runs inside your operating system and has access to your files by default.

What makes this week's story different from a standard product announcement is the installation method. Microsoft calls it a "background deployment." Starting in mid-June and running through mid-July, the Copilot app is being automatically installed on eligible commercial Windows 11 PCs during routine software updates. You don't click yes. You don't get a prompt. [VERIFY THIS: confirm whether a notification appears or whether the install is truly silent] The app appears.

The opt-out exists, but it wasn't designed for you. Companies with IT administrators — dedicated tech managers who oversee devices — were given the ability to block the rollout before it reached employees' machines. Individual users, freelancers, and small business owners with personal Microsoft 365 subscriptions were not given an equivalent option. [VERIFY THIS: confirm there is no individual-level control, only admin-level opt-out] If you don't have an IT department, which most people don't, the decision was already made.

There is one group this doesn't apply to: users in the European Economic Area, which includes EU member states plus Norway, Iceland, and Liechtenstein. Microsoft exempted them entirely. Forcing software onto someone's device without consent would violate GDPR and EU consumer protection law, and Microsoft knows it. The fact that they carved out that region while continuing everywhere else is not a technicality. It's an admission about what kind of behavior requires a legal consequence to stop.

An earlier version of this rollout was suspended after reports of bugs — including one that reportedly allowed Copilot to access confidential emails. Here's the part that most coverage on this story skips: uninstalling an application that Microsoft installed without your permission is completely legal, and not technically difficult.

Open the Start menu. Go to Settings, then Apps, then Installed apps. Search for "Copilot." If it's there and you didn't put it there, you can remove it from that same screen.
