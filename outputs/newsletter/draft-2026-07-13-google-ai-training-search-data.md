In June, Google sent an email to some users about a change to its privacy settings. Not a banner. Not a pop-up when you opened Google. An email, subject line something about "Search Services," easy to miss or skip.

The change it described: photos, files, and audio recordings you upload to Google Search tools are now stored in a new category and used to improve Google's AI models. By default. Unless you turn it off.

**What "Search Services History" actually is**

Google has been storing your search history for years — that's not new. What changed is that they created a separate category for a specific kind of data: media you upload while using Google's search products.

The new "Search Services History" setting applies to Google Search, Google Maps, Google Shopping, Google Flights, Google Hotels, Google Translate, and Google News. It also covers AI Mode — Google's AI-powered search interface — and any "Ask Maps" threads where you upload files or photos.

**Why this is different from your regular search history**

Most people have made some kind of uneasy peace with the idea that their Google searches are being collected. Search text feels like the least private of their digital data — just words typed into a box.

The expansion to images, audio, and uploaded files is meaningfully different. Not because the principle changed, but because the richness of the data did.

A voice search captures your voice, your environment, the background sounds of your home or car. A photo captures more than what you intended to search for — it captures context, location data embedded in the file, sometimes other people. A document you upload might contain financial information, health information, or client work.

Google is now treating all of that the same way it treats a search for "best pizza near me." [YOUR TAKE ON THIS — is this a reasonable extension of their existing model, or does it cross a line? Your opinion here will be the most-shared part of this piece]

The non-obvious piece: Google framed this change as giving users "more control." That framing is technically accurate — they created a new setting. But "more control" doesn't mean less collection. It means they started doing something new and gave you a way to stop it after the fact.

**What you can actually do right now**

The opt-out is genuinely straightforward once you find the page. Finding the page is the whole challenge.

Go to **myactivity.google.com**. Sign in. Find the "Search Services History" tab. You have two options:

Turn it off entirely — this stops both saving and AI training from this category of data.

Or, if you want to keep your text search history for your own use while stopping image and audio collection, find the "Save Media" checkbox inside the Search Services History setting and uncheck it specifically.

Important: turning this off doesn't delete data Google has already collected. If you want to clear what's been stored, you'll need to hit the delete option separately on the same page. The "stop collecting going forward" and "delete what's there now" are two distinct actions. **The pattern behind the pattern**

Google is not doing something uniquely unusual here. This is the established playbook: update what you're collecting, announce it in privacy policy language, make it opt-out rather than opt-in, and see who notices.

Meta does the same thing. Microsoft does the same thing. The difference is that Google sits in a position most people treat as ambient infrastructure — Search isn't something you sign up for the way you sign up for Instagram. You use it the way you use a road. And roads don't typically train AI on where you've been.

What regulators are starting to catch up to: the EU AI Act, taking effect August 2, 2026, includes requirements around how companies communicate AI use to users. That won't fix opt-out defaults overnight, but it does mean the legal exposure for obscuring these changes is getting larger. US law has no equivalent yet.
