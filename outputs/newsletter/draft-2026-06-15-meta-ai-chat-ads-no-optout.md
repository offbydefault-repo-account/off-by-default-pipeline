## DRAFT BODY

If you've ever asked Meta AI a question on Facebook, Instagram, or WhatsApp, that conversation didn't stay between you and the chatbot. It became an advertising signal.

Not in a vague "they know your interests" way. In a specific "you asked about hiking boots so now you're seeing ads for outdoor gear in your feed" way. Meta added this to its privacy policy in December 2025. It rolled out quietly. No announcement. No pop-up. No choice.

This is where you'd normally hear "but you agreed to the Terms of Service." And technically, you did. But if you didn't read 30 pages of legalese before clicking Accept, you weren't alone — and the company knew that when they wrote it that way. [HUMOR OPPORTUNITY? — FLAG FOR REVIEW]

Meta AI is built into Facebook, Instagram, and WhatsApp. You might have used it without thinking much of it — searching for a restaurant, getting help writing a caption, asking for recipe ideas. It feels conversational. It doesn't feel like filling out a form for an advertiser.

That's exactly what it became. What they don't say: there's no audit trail you can check, and no way to verify on your end what counted as sensitive and what didn't.

Here's the part most coverage skips: this matters differently depending on where you live.

If you're in the European Union, the United Kingdom, or South Korea, you can opt out of this. Regulators in those places require it. If you're in the United States, there is no equivalent right.

There is a setting — buried several layers deep in your privacy options, labeled something like AI Data Usage — but it offers limited control at best. [VERIFY THIS — confirm what the US setting actually covers and what it doesn't stop] It doesn't give you the same protection European users have. The difference isn't a technical limitation. The technology to offer the same opt-out to American users already exists. Meta built it for Europe. They chose not to offer it here.

This is the non-obvious thing worth sitting with: data rights in the US are not equal to data rights in the EU. For years that difference was abstract. Now it's concrete. A Meta AI user in Berlin can opt out of having their conversations used to sell them things. A Meta AI user in Birmingham, Alabama cannot.

---

The broader pattern is worth naming, even if it's uncomfortable.

Meta is not uniquely villainous here. Google has AI embedded in Gmail, Google Docs, and Search. Microsoft has Copilot in Word and Outlook. Amazon has Alexa in millions of homes. Every one of them has a privacy policy describing how your interactions with their AI get used. Meta's version is just more direct — conversations become ad signals, stated plainly, with no US opt-out.

The chatbot framing does real work here. When you're shopping online, most people understand they're generating data. When you're having what feels like a conversation, the data collection doesn't feel the same way — even if the outcome is similar. [YOUR TAKE ON THIS — does that distinction matter?
