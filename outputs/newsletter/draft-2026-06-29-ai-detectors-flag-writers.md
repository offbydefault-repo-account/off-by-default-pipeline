**Topic:** AI writing detectors are flagging real writers as AI — and the math says it can't be fixed

---

**Subject line A (curiosity gap):** The tool that's supposed to catch AI writing keeps flagging real writers instead.
**Subject line B (direct):** AI writing detectors are broken — and the false positive problem is mathematically unfixable.
**One-sentence social distribution summary:** New research proves that AI text detectors systematically flag polished human writing as AI-generated — and the problem cannot be engineered away, only misapplied.

---

A writer won a short story competition in the UK this month. Her entry was run through an AI detection tool by the organizers. It came back 100 percent machine-generated. She wrote every word herself.

This is not an outlier story. Publishers have canceled contracts with human authors after getting similar scores. Universities have opened misconduct proceedings against students who didn't use AI at all. A federal judge ruled in February 2026 that one such accusation was "without merit." At least five federal lawsuits from falsely flagged students are now working their way through the courts.

The instinct when you hear this is to assume it's a calibration problem — that someone just needs to train the tool better. New research published this week says that instinct is wrong. And understanding why it's wrong matters more than knowing what any tool scored you.

---

AI text detectors work by looking for statistical patterns. AI writing has recognizable tendencies: controlled vocabulary, consistent sentence structure, logical transitions, predictable word choices. Detectors learn to spot these patterns and return a probability estimate of whether text was machine-generated.

The problem is that skilled human writers produce the same patterns. Spend years at your craft — trimming excess, finding a consistent voice, cleaning up your sentences — and your writing looks, statistically, a lot like well-trained AI output. Not because you used AI. Because you got good at writing.

The false positive problem is not evenly distributed.

If you write in English as a second language and you write it carefully — structured, controlled, free of colloquialisms — you are significantly more likely to be flagged than a native English speaker who writes loosely. Stanford researchers documented a 61 percent false positive rate for essays written by Chinese students on TOEFL tests, compared with a 5 percent rate for essays from native speakers on the same tests. [VERIFY THIS — confirm this is Stanford research and the specific percentages]

The tools penalize precision. They penalize writers from non-English-speaking backgrounds who have worked hardest to express themselves clearly. The writers least likely to have cheated — those who've spent years developing a disciplined writing practice — are the ones most likely to be accused of it.

Here is what this means practically if you write anything that anyone evaluates.

Document your process now, before you need to. Keep your drafts. Save version history — Google Docs records every revision with timestamps, Word's tracked changes does the same, Scrivener keeps snapshots of your project. If your work is ever challenged, your defense will not be a competing score from a different tool. It will be your process, your drafts, your timestamps.

This is not paranoid. It is the same reasoning that leads a financial professional to keep receipts. An accusation doesn't have to be correct to cost you time, money, and reputation to disprove.

If you are in a position to influence how these tools are used — as a teacher, an editor, a publisher, a competition organizer — the research is now explicit enough that deploying AI detection for high-stakes decisions is indefensible. The tools will tell you someone cheated when they didn't. That's already documented in federal court records.
