---

You didn't sign up to train an AI.

You posted photos of your work, wrote blog entries about your process, shared your thoughts in public spaces online. Those things existed somewhere on the internet, attached to your name, minding their own business. You didn't put them there for OpenAI or Google or Meta. You put them there for the people you were talking to.

But that's what happened. Last week, Amnesty International released a detailed briefing on exactly this. The title is "Unlawful by Design." The argument isn't that AI companies cut a few corners or made some mistakes. It's that the entire architecture of how these systems were built is structurally incompatible with basic privacy rights — and that this wasn't an oversight. It was the plan.

If you've had a quiet sense that something was taken from you — that your work ended up somewhere you never agreed to — this report is saying you were right to feel that way.

---

What the report actually covers is worth understanding in plain terms, because the headlines have been doing it no favors.

The major AI models powering the tools most people use every day — OpenAI's GPT series (which runs ChatGPT), Google's Gemini, Meta's Llama, DeepSeek, Midjourney, and Stable Diffusion — were trained on datasets assembled through mass web scraping. Automated systems crawled the internet and collected enormous quantities of text, images, and other content to feed the models during training.

This isn't a secret. The companies have generally acknowledged that their models were trained on "publicly available internet data."

What Amnesty is saying — and this is the specific claim that makes this report different from general criticism — is that "publicly available" does not mean "consented to for this purpose." Under international human rights law, the mass collection of billions of people's personal content without individual knowledge or consent constitutes a privacy violation regardless of whether that content was technically accessible to the public.

The phrase "unlawful by design" means the problem isn't incidental. It isn't a data breach. It isn't a misconfigured setting or a rogue contractor. The scraping and collection is how these systems had to be built to reach the scale they operate at. The privacy violation isn't a side effect of how they were built. It's a prerequisite.

There's a version of this argument you might have already heard and quietly dismissed: "Well, I posted things publicly. What did I expect?"

That framing sounds reasonable until you look at what it actually implies. Posting your portfolio publicly isn't the same as consenting to every tech company in existence being allowed to harvest it in bulk — automatically, without contact, without payment, without any opportunity to say no — to train commercial products they'll sell back to you.

This is the mechanism behind that feeling. It would land well to name it specifically.]

Amnesty also raises a point that goes beyond copyright and ownership: when people know that everything they write publicly might be scraped and used to train systems they'll later interact with, it changes what they're willing to say. The chilling effect on creative output and free expression is itself a harm — not just a philosophical concern, but something the report frames as a specific human rights impact.

The counterintuitive observation here: the problem isn't that AI tools exist. It's that the version of AI we got is the version that could be built for free, by taking without asking. A version that required licensing, consent, and compensation would have cost more and been slower to arrive. The choice to build it this way was a business decision, not a technical inevitability.

---

So what can you actually do with this information?

The honest answer is: not much, immediately, as an individual. If you're in the EU, you have the clearest legal path — the EU Copyright Directive now allows rights holders to formally reserve their work from AI training, and the EU AI Act requires developers to respect those reservations. In the UK, the government recently reversed a plan that would have made the situation actively worse for creators. In the US, there's pending bipartisan legislation (the Dean/Moran bill) that would give creators meaningful legal access to courts when their work is used without authorization, but it hasn't passed yet. You can't get your data back. It's in the training sets. What you can do is make choices going forward about which tools you use and how you use them — and make those choices with accurate information instead of vague unease.
