## Draft

If you've ever held your phone up to a plant to find out what it is, pointed your camera at a restaurant menu to read it in English, or asked Google a question out loud while your hands were full — you've been doing something that Google has been using for AI training since June.

You were sent an email about it. Most people didn't open it.

In June 2026, Google updated its privacy settings for Search-related services. The update said that photos you send through Google Lens, voice recordings from voice queries, files you upload to Google Translate, and similar search inputs would now be saved and used to train Google's AI models. By default. No opt-in step, no checkbox, no notification that asked you to click anything. The update went out via email in the format — a long policy notice with a legal header — that most of us have been trained to close without reading.

Before we go further, I want to name the thing that will confuse most people about this story: this is not about Google Photos. Your camera roll, your personal photo library, the memories Google has been organizing with little "On this day" notifications — that's explicitly excluded from what changed. Google Photos is not affected.

What changed is the search input side. The photo you held up to Lens to identify a mushroom. The voice query you made while cooking. The document you sent to Google Translate because you couldn't read the contract your landlord gave you. Those are task inputs — things you were using Google as a live tool to process, not storing, not sharing with anyone. And now they're also training data.

That distinction is important because those inputs are often more sensitive than what's in your camera roll. You didn't choose to archive them. You were using Google as a utility, the same way you'd use a calculator. And the information you used the tool on — the rash, the letter from the IRS, the name on a prescription bottle — wasn't something you were trying to document. You were trying to understand it. The affected inputs include: photos processed through Google Lens; audio from spoken voice searches; files uploaded to Google Translate; and "similar inputs" sent through Google's search tools. If you search only by typing, you're not affected by the media-capture portion. But most people who use a smartphone use at least one of these tools regularly.

[PERSONAL EXAMPLE]

Here's the part that's worth sitting with: Google told people about this in June. Via email. In a policy update notification that looked just like the dozens of others that arrive each year. So technically, you were informed. Technically, this was disclosed.

But disclosure that's designed to be missed is doing different work than disclosure that's designed to inform. The timing, the format, and the placement of the opt-out are not accidents — they're a system. The system is very efficient at producing training data from people who were trying to accomplish something else.

The opt-out does exist. It takes about three minutes to use. It's not hidden exactly — it's just not where you'd look unless you already knew it was there.

Here is how to use it. Go to myactivity.google.com and sign in. Find the section called "Search Services History." Inside it, look for a setting called "Save Media." Turn that off. That stops Google from retaining and using the photos, audio, and files you send through its search tools for AI training going forward.

If you want to keep your search history for your own reference but stop only the media capture specifically, the Save Media toggle is the right one. If you want to go further, turning off Web and App Activity entirely gives you more protection, but it also reduces how personalized Google's results are. That's a trade-off worth thinking about, not a universal recommendation. One worth knowing: this opt-out applies going forward from when you change the setting. It does not retroactively delete media inputs that were already captured before you change it. If that matters to you, there's a separate process to request deletion of prior data through Google's My Activity dashboard.

---

The counterintuitive thing about this story — the thing worth carrying with you — is that Google's handling of it is not unusual. It's industry standard. Every major platform now assumes you're opted in until you opt out, and tells you about it in the format least likely to get your attention. The people who notice are the ones who follow tech news for a living. Everyone else just uses the product.

The next time a policy update email arrives from any platform — Google, Meta, Adobe, Microsoft, whoever — pause before closing it. Scan for the word "default." Look for anything that mentions what behavior is being "enabled" or "activated." The emails that contain something like this are designed to look exactly like the ones that don't.
