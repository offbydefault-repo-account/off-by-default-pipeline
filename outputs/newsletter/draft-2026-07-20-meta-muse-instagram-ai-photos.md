## Draft

On July 7, Meta launched something called Muse Image. If you have a public Instagram account, it was already using your photos before most people had heard the name.

The feature worked like this: any Instagram user could type your username into a prompt and generate AI images using your face and your photos as source material. Not copies of your photos — new images, made in whatever style or scenario the person typing wanted. You were opted in by default. You were not notified. You had to find the opt-out yourself, if you even knew to look.

SAG-AFTRA noticed. Three days after launch, on July 10, the union issued a public statement telling its members — and all Instagram users — to opt out immediately. "Take action to protect your likeness" was their headline. CAA called it a consent violation. By July 13, Meta had pulled the feature from Instagram.

Six days from launch to removal.

If you're thinking "good, problem solved" — I want to gently slow that down.

Meta did not remove Muse Image. They removed one specific version of it. The tool still runs inside the Meta AI app and inside WhatsApp. What they pulled was the feature that allowed your public Instagram account to be tagged as a reference source by strangers on Instagram. The broader product is still live. The underlying logic hasn't changed.

And the opt-out is still there, which means the default is still opt-in.

[YOUR TAKE ON THIS]

Here's the part that got less coverage than it should have: Meta didn't launch Muse Image expecting it to be controversial. They launched it assuming that this is what "public" means now — that if your account is visible to the world, your photos are reusable as AI training and reference material by anyone with access to their tools.

There's a real difference between a photo that's accessible to your audience and a photo being used as reference material for AI images a stranger generates. You can post a photo of yourself at your studio, or at your book event, or at your desk, for your followers, and not intend it as raw material for anyone who wants to generate new images of you in scenarios you never agreed to. Accessible and reusable are not the same thing. You chose one. You didn't sign up for the other.

Platforms have been quietly collapsing that distinction for a while. Muse Image just made it impossible to ignore.

[PERSONAL EXAMPLE]

The speed of the rollback matters. Six days is fast. Faster than most platforms walk back a bad call. And the reason it moved that fast wasn't because Meta had a change of heart — it's because the people most affected, the professional creators and actors who've spent years thinking about likeness rights and consent, saw it immediately and said so loudly enough that it cost Meta more to keep it than to pull it.

That's worth understanding as a model. You don't have to be a lawyer or a technologist to participate in that kind of pressure. You have to know what's happening. That's the only real requirement.

So what do you do with this?

First, and most immediately: if you have a public Instagram account, go into your settings and find the AI data controls. Meta has an opt-out for training on your content. On the mobile app, go to Settings > Privacy, then look for the section on data sharing or AI content — search the settings for "AI" if you can't find it quickly. Turn off whatever opt-in you find there. [VERIFY THIS — the exact path may have shifted after the Muse Image rollback; confirm current location in both iOS and Android app versions]

Second: consider whether your account actually needs to be public. Private accounts were excluded from Muse Image's defaults entirely. If you've been thinking about switching and haven't gotten around to it, this week gave you a concrete reason to make the call.

Third, and this is the one that applies beyond Instagram: pay attention to product announcements that bury the actual news. When any major platform says "we're rolling out a new creative tool," the useful question isn't "what can I do with it" — it's "what can it do with me, and am I opted in already?"
