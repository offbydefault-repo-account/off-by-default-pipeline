For four days last week, anyone on Instagram could type your name into an AI tool and get realistic photos of you doing things you never did.

They didn't need your permission. They didn't need to tell you. If your account was public, you were automatically in — and the only way to stop it was to find a setting you didn't know existed and turn it off yourself.

That feature is now gone. Meta pulled Muse Image on July 10th, four days after launch, after SAG-AFTRA, Creative Artists Agency, and enough public pressure to make the headlines. **What Muse Image actually was**

Meta launched Muse Image on July 8 as a "creative partner" — an AI photo generator you could use to make images for your social media pages. On paper, it sounds like every other AI image tool.

The piece that caused the firestorm: anyone could @-mention a public Instagram account inside a prompt, and the AI would use that person's public photos to generate new images. Their face. Their likeness. Other people visible in those photos — including children.

You were not notified when this happened. You did not give consent. You were automatically in.

For the 96 hours that Muse Image's @-mention feature was live, any adult with a public Instagram account could have had AI images generated of them by a complete stranger with no notification and no immediate legal remedy. **Why the backlash landed so fast**

SAG-AFTRA moved within 48 hours. The union put out a statement calling for nothing less than a "clear and conspicuous opt-in" — meaning your permission, asked for in advance, not buried in settings after the fact. Creative Artists Agency joined. India's government raised formal challenges.

The entertainment industry has been watching this playbook closely. [HUMOR OPPORTUNITY? — FLAG FOR REVIEW — the "fourth time in two years" line below has some dark comedic potential if the newsletter's voice supports it]

This is the fourth time in less than two years that Meta introduced a product built on the same false premise: that silence equals consent. They launch. They absorb the backlash. They adjust or retract. Then they try again, slightly differently.

This time, the pattern held. Meta paused the @-mention feature — but only on Instagram. Muse Image is still live on WhatsApp and the Meta AI app. The feature just no longer lets you use other people's Instagram accounts as source material on Instagram specifically.

Which is a bit like removing the one door you knew about while leaving the rest of the house open.

---

**What this actually means for you**

If your Instagram account is public right now, it is still publicly accessible to AI systems — not just Meta's. Other companies' tools can scrape public profiles. Muse Image's removal from Instagram doesn't change the underlying calculus: a public account means your photos are available to anyone building a tool to use them.

This is not a hypothetical. It happened at scale last week, and the company doing it had the resources and legal team to move faster than any regulator could.

Here's the one thing to sit with: Meta didn't do anything secretly. Muse Image was announced publicly. The feature was described in marketing copy. The opt-out existed. What they designed was a system where your consent was assumed unless you withdrew it — and most people didn't know they'd consented to anything.

That's the gap between "legal" and "acceptable" that this conversation keeps circling back to. [YOUR TAKE ON THIS — this is a genuine philosophical question worth your voice here]

**What you can actually do**

If you want to keep your Instagram account public — for your work, your portfolio, your audience — there's no way to opt out of all AI training across all companies simultaneously. What you can do is reduce your exposure on Meta's platforms specifically.

Go to your Meta AI settings and look for anything related to AI image generation or content that references your account. If you've been using a public account, consider whether the discoverability tradeoff is still worth it for the kinds of photos you're posting.
