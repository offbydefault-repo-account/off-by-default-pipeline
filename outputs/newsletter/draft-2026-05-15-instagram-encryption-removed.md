## DRAFT BODY

On May 8, a privacy protection quietly disappeared from your Instagram direct messages. Most people using the app that day had no idea it had been there in the first place. That's not a coincidence. Meta never told them.

End-to-end encryption for Instagram DMs had existed since 2023 — three years of a feature that was real, that worked, and that virtually no one knew about because Meta made no effort to surface it. It was an optional setting buried inside individual conversation threads, requiring a four-step process to enable, reset to off every time you started a new conversation with a new person.

When Meta removed it, their explanation was that adoption had been too low. [HUMOR OPPORTUNITY? — FLAG FOR REVIEW]

---

**What end-to-end encryption actually means**

End-to-end encryption means that only the two people in a conversation can read it. Not the platform, not the company's servers, not advertisers, and not a government with a court order — at least not without seizing the actual device. When messages are encrypted end-to-end, the service carrying them is like a sealed envelope rather than a postcard. Instagram was the postal worker. For three years, some of your envelopes were sealed. Now they're not.

When encryption is absent, the platform can read the content of your messages. Meta uses this access for content moderation — flagging messages that violate policies — and for ad targeting. Your conversations inform what you're shown. That's not a conspiracy theory; it's how the business model works.

For most everyday messaging — weekend plans, compliments on someone's work, questions about an event — this probably feels like a low-stakes change. But the people for whom this matters most are exactly the people who should know it happened: journalists communicating with sources, artists discussing unreleased work, people in complicated personal situations, anyone who assumed that a private message on Instagram was, in any meaningful sense, private.

---

**How Meta pulled off the "nobody used it" defense**

Here is a precise account of how Instagram's end-to-end encryption worked in practice before May 8. You opened a conversation. You tapped a settings icon. You found the encryption option. You enabled it for that conversation. You did this again for every subsequent new conversation. If you forgot, the conversation was unencrypted.

Meta never sent a push notification that this feature existed. It was never mentioned in onboarding for new users. It never appeared as a suggested setting or a prompt. It was not on by default. It was not explained in any Help Center article that surfaces when you search "Instagram DMs." It existed, technically, but it was designed to be invisible.

Then Meta looked at the resulting adoption numbers — which were very low, because almost no one knew the feature was there — and announced that those numbers proved users didn't want encryption. Then they removed it.

This is a specific kind of argument. It sounds like evidence. It is not evidence. Low adoption of a feature you deliberately hide does not demonstrate user preference for the unencrypted alternative. It demonstrates that defaults matter enormously, and that Meta understood exactly what they were doing when they chose the defaults they chose. Meta pointed users who want encrypted messaging toward WhatsApp. WhatsApp is also owned by Meta, and its end-to-end encryption is genuine and on by default — which makes the Instagram situation even more revealing. If Meta can build encrypted messaging into one product and default it to on, the decision not to do so in another product is a choice.

**What the Electronic Frontier Foundation said (and why it matters)**

The EFF published a response they titled "Broken Promises: RIP Instagram's End-to-End Encrypted DMs." Their language was pointed: "Encryption is not just 'a feature.' It is fundamental to safety and the exercise of human rights."

That framing is worth taking seriously rather than setting aside as advocacy-speak. Encryption protects not just privacy in the abstract but specific kinds of people in specific kinds of situations: abuse survivors in contact with support networks, LGBTQ+ users in unsupportive environments, creators who discuss unreleased work under NDA, anyone in a legal dispute involving communication records.

The EFF's criticism isn't that Meta built something and then changed it. Companies change products. The criticism is the specific arc: build a privacy feature, make it effectively invisible, measure the invisible thing's low usage, cite that usage as justification for removal. The pattern is its own message. [YOUR TAKE ON THIS]

**What you can actually do**

If you use Instagram DMs for anything you'd be uncomfortable seeing in a screenshot or a legal discovery process: stop, or treat them accordingly.

The alternative Meta itself recommends is WhatsApp. It's owned by the same company, and if that matters to you, it's a reasonable concern. The metadata — who you're talking to, how often, for how long — is still visible to Meta, even if the message content isn't.

Signal is the option for people who want no Meta involvement at all. It is free, it has been audited by security researchers, and it does not belong to a company with an advertising business model. For Instagram DMs specifically: treat them like text messages — useful for logistics, not appropriate for anything genuinely sensitive. That has always been the technically accurate position. Now it's just more visibly true.

**One recommendation:**
If you want to have encrypted conversations and you're not ready to move everyone you know to a new app, WhatsApp's encryption is meaningful. If you're starting from scratch: Signal. Either way, Instagram DMs are now the wrong place for anything you'd rather keep between the two of you.

**Closing line:**
The next time a platform cites "low adoption" to justify removing a privacy feature, ask who controlled the defaults — and how long they'd controlled them.
