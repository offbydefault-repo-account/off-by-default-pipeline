## HOOK — First 15 seconds

Last week, Amnesty International released a report calling ChatGPT, Gemini, Llama, Midjourney, and Stable Diffusion "unlawful by design."

That's not a complaint. That's a specific legal and human rights claim. Here's what they actually mean — and why it's different from everything you've heard before.

---

## SETUP

The question of where AI training data comes from isn't new. Artists, writers, and photographers have been asking since 2022. Courts have been sorting through lawsuits. Journalists have written about it.

What's new is this: a major international human rights organization did a detailed investigation, named specific companies, named specific legal frameworks, and concluded that the problem isn't a mistake or an oversight.

The title of the report is "Unlawful by Design." That phrase is doing work. It isn't saying there was an accident. It's saying the design requires the violation — that these systems couldn't have been built at this scale any other way.

[SOURCE NEEDED: full citation — "Unlawful by Design: Exposing the Human Rights Costs of Generative AI," Amnesty International, published May 2026]

---

## EXPLANATION

Here's how these AI models were trained. Companies assembled massive datasets — hundreds of billions of words, hundreds of millions of images — by scraping the internet. Automated systems crawled public websites, social media platforms, blogs, forums, and creative portfolios and collected everything they could reach.

The companies have generally acknowledged this. They call it using "publicly available data."

Amnesty's argument is that "publicly available" and "consented to for this purpose" are not the same thing. Under international human rights law, the mass collection of personal content — your posts, your photos, your publicly visible writing — without individual knowledge or consent is a privacy violation at scale, regardless of whether the content was technically accessible to the public.

The briefing specifically examined the training data behind GPT (which runs ChatGPT), Google's Gemini, Meta's Llama, DeepSeek, Midjourney, and Stable Diffusion. It found that the data collection practices behind all of them conflict with international human rights standards — specifically around privacy, freedom of expression, and the right to control your own information.

---

## NUANCE — The thing most explainer videos on this topic skip

The common defense of AI training practices sounds like this: "You posted things publicly. What did you expect?"

And there's a surface-level version of that which sounds reasonable — if you publish something on the internet, people can read it.

But scale changes the nature of the act. There's a meaningful difference between "people can read your blog" and "a company harvests your entire blog automatically, along with hundreds of millions of others, to train commercial products they'll sell back to you at a subscription fee."

The first is how the internet was supposed to work. The second is something else.

Here's the part most coverage misses entirely: Amnesty's report isn't only about data ownership and copyright. It's also about what this does to expression. When people know — or gradually figure out — that everything they write publicly might be scraped and used to train systems they'll later interact with, it changes what they're willing to say. That's a chilling effect on free expression, and the report frames it as a specific, measurable human rights harm.

The counterintuitive observation here: the version of AI we got is the version that could be built cheapest and fastest — by taking training data without asking. A version that required licensing, consent, and compensation would have been slower and more expensive. The choice to build it this way was a business decision. It was made by people who had other options.

## TAKEAWAY

Here's what this means practically, depending on where you are.

If you're in the EU: you now have the clearest legal protections. The EU Copyright Directive lets rights holders formally reserve their work from AI training, and the EU AI Act requires developers to honor those reservations. The opt-out mechanism exists and is enforceable.

If you're in the UK: the government recently reversed a plan that would have made the situation worse for creators — your work isn't automatically fair game, but the positive protection isn't fully built yet either.

If you're in the US: there's bipartisan legislation pending (the Dean/Moran bill) to give creators legal standing when their work is used without authorization, but it hasn't passed.

For most people watching this: the data that was scraped is already in the training sets. You can't retrieve it.

What you can do is use these tools with accurate information about what they are and how they were built — and pay attention to which companies, going forward, are asking permission before adding your work to the next model.

Amnesty's full report is at amnesty.org. It's called "Unlawful by Design." It's readable and thorough and worth your time.
