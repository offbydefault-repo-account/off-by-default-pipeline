### HOOK — First 15 seconds

Quick question.

When's the last time you pointed your phone at something to find out what it was? Or asked Google a question out loud while you were driving or cooking?

If you've done either of those in the last few weeks, there's a good chance Google has been using that interaction for AI training. Since June. And most people have no idea — because the announcement went out in the format most likely to be ignored.

### SETUP

In June 2026, Google updated its privacy settings for its search-related services. They sent an email about it. It looked like every other policy notification email: long, formal, legal header, the word "update" in the subject line.

Most people closed it.

But inside was something significant: a change to what Google keeps, and what it does with what it keeps, from the media you send through its search tools. Photos, voice recordings, uploaded files — now captured for AI model training. By default. No checkbox. No opt-in.

TechCrunch flagged it on July 6 and called it an under-the-radar update. That's when most people heard about it. [VERIFY THIS]

### EXPLANATION

Let me be clear about what this covers and what it doesn't, because the confusion is understandable.

This is not about Google Photos. Your camera roll, your backed-up pictures, the photo albums Google organizes with little "On this day" prompts — that's explicitly excluded from what changed. If you're worried about your personal photo library, you can set that aside.

What changed is the search input side.

The photo you held up to Google Lens to identify a plant, or a mushroom, or a skin rash you weren't sure about. The voice query you sent while your hands were full. The document you uploaded to Google Translate because you couldn't read what it said. Those are task inputs — things you sent to Google as live tool queries, not as things you wanted to save.

And those inputs are now being retained and used to train Google's AI models.

[YOUR TAKE ON THIS — delivery note: this is a good spot to be direct, no hedging]

Here's why that distinction matters more than it might seem.

The things you run through search tools are often more sensitive than what's in your photo library. Your camera roll is mostly vacation photos and screenshots. Your Lens queries might be: a rash on your kid's arm, a letter from the IRS you needed to parse, a prescription label you couldn't read, a product you were suspicious of. Those are questions, not memories. You weren't trying to document them. You were trying to understand them.

Using Google as a tool to answer a question is a different act than sharing a photo. The thing you're using the tool on isn't necessarily something you'd be comfortable handing over to anyone. But that's what the default change means.

### NUANCE — The thing most explainer videos on this topic skip

The June email isn't the interesting part of this story.

The interesting part is what the email was designed to accomplish. Google disclosed this change. Technically. In a policy notification. The design of that notification — the format, the placement, the subject line — is calibrated for low engagement. A change that most people miss is still, legally, a change that most people were told about.

This is not unique to Google. Every major platform now assumes you're opted in until you opt out, and tells you about it in the format least likely to cause you to change anything. The people who notice are the ones who follow tech news for a living. Everyone else just uses the product.

The opt-out exists. It takes three minutes. But it requires knowing the question exists before you know to ask it.

---

### TAKEAWAY

Here's what to do.

Go to myactivity.google.com. Sign in. Look for Search Services History. Inside it, find "Save Media." Turn it off.

That stops Google from retaining and using the photos, audio, and files you send through search tools for AI training going forward. It does not affect Google Photos. It does not affect how your search results are personalized. It just removes your search media inputs from the training pipeline.

[VERIFY THIS — confirm the exact path and toggle name at myactivity.google.com is current as of July 2026]

It won't erase anything already captured. If you want to request deletion of prior data, there's a separate process through the My Activity dashboard — worth a follow-up if that matters to you.

One habit worth building out of this: next time any platform sends you a policy update email, scan it for the word "default." If something is changing how the platform handles your data, the word default will usually appear somewhere in there. It's not foolproof, but it's a filter that catches more than "update" alone.

The change in June was real. The opt-out is real. The email was real. The question is whether you were in a position to act on it when it arrived — and if not, how to put yourself in that position next time.
