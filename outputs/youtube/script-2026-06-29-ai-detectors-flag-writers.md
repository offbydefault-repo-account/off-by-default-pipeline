## SETUP

AI text detection tools are everywhere now. Publishers use them. Universities use them. Writing competitions use them. The idea is: you run a piece of writing through the tool, it tells you whether a human or an AI wrote it, and you act on that.

The problem is the "act on that" part. Because the information is not reliable.

Five federal lawsuits have been filed by students falsely accused based on AI detection scores. A federal judge called one accusation "without merit." Publishers have canceled contracts with human authors. More than 40 universities have dropped or restricted these tools after legal warnings from faculty.

## EXPLANATION

How do these detectors work? They look for statistical patterns. AI writing has recognizable tendencies — consistent sentence structure, controlled vocabulary, logical transitions, predictable word choices. The tool learns to recognize those patterns and returns a probability score.

The flaw: those patterns also appear in skilled human writing.

Spend years developing your craft — cutting clutter, finding your voice, tightening your sentences — and your writing looks, statistically, a lot like well-trained AI output. Not because you're using AI. Because you got good.

The research published this week showed, using formal probability theory, that any detector with real detection power will necessarily generate false positives among skilled writers whose prose overlaps statistically with AI output. Not occasionally — necessarily. As a mathematical requirement. You cannot build a system that reliably catches AI writing without also flagging good human writing, because the statistical fingerprints overlap. [VERIFY THIS — confirm the specific mathematical claim made in the paper]

More training data doesn't fix this. Better algorithms don't fix this. The thing these tools are looking for doesn't belong exclusively to AI.

## NUANCE — the thing most explainer videos skip

The false positive problem doesn't hit everyone the same way.

If English is your second language and you write it carefully — structured sentences, no colloquialisms, controlled vocabulary — you are statistically more likely to be flagged than a native English speaker who writes loosely. Stanford researchers documented a 61 percent false positive rate for Chinese students writing TOEFL essays in English. Five percent for native speakers on the same tests. So the tool most aggressively penalizes people who've worked hardest to write well in a second language. The discipline that marks a serious writer becomes evidence of machine output. That's not a bug that can be patched. That's the math.

One more: NeurIPS — one of the world's most prestigious AI research conferences — rejected 178 submitted papers this year after a detection tool flagged them as 100 percent machine-generated. ## TAKEAWAY

If you're a writer: document your process. Keep your drafts. Use version history — Google Docs tracks every revision with timestamps, Word has tracked changes, Scrivener has snapshots. If your work is ever challenged, your defense is your process. Not a competing score from a different tool.

If you're in a position to decide how these tools get used — teacher, editor, publisher, competition organizer — the research is now explicit: using AI detection for high-stakes decisions means you will falsely accuse real writers. That's already in federal court records. Knowing that and still using the tools is a choice.

A tool that penalizes you for writing well isn't protecting integrity. It's just punishing skill.
