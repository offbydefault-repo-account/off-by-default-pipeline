### HOOK — First 15 seconds

Six days.

That's how long Meta's Muse Image ran on Instagram before they pulled it. In those six days, anyone could take your public Instagram photos and use them to generate AI images of you — in whatever style, whatever scenario, whatever context they wanted.

You didn't have to agree. You didn't have to know.

And here's the thing: they pulled the Instagram piece. They didn't pull the tool. And the reason it got pulled isn't the reason you'd hope for.

---

### SETUP

On July 7, Meta launched Muse Image. It's an AI image generation tool, and it's built into several Meta products. On Instagram, it had a specific feature: you could tag any public account's username in a prompt, and Meta's AI would generate new images using that account's photos as visual reference.

Your face. Your aesthetic. Your style. Reusable as raw material by anyone who typed your handle into a box.

You were opted in automatically if your account was public. There was no announcement to the people whose photos were being used. You had to find the opt-out yourself, which means you had to know to look for it.

---

### EXPLANATION

SAG-AFTRA — the actors' union — issued a statement on July 10, three days after launch. Their recommendation: opt out immediately. "Take action to protect your likeness." CAA, one of the largest talent agencies in Hollywood, called for the feature to be removed entirely.

Meta pulled the Instagram integration on July 13. Six days.

But here is what they actually did: they removed the specific part of Muse Image that let users tag Instagram accounts as image sources. Muse Image itself is still running in the Meta AI app and in WhatsApp. The broader tool — and the logic behind it — is still there.

The opt-out that existed before the rollback is still there, which means the default is still opt-in.

### NUANCE — The thing most coverage of this story missed

The speed of the rollback made it look like the system worked. Creator backlash, union stepped in, platform backed down. Win.

I'd push back on that framing.

What got pulled was the visibility of the problem. The Instagram tagging feature was the part people could see and point to — "look, this specific thing is using my specific photos." That's gone from Instagram.

But the underlying assumption behind the launch hasn't changed. The assumption is: if your Instagram account is public, your photos are reusable. Not just viewable by your audience — reusable as reference material by anyone with access to Meta's tools.

There's a real difference between those two things. A photo you posted for your followers is not automatically a photo you offered as training data for someone else's image generation session. Accessible is not reusable. Platforms have been treating them as synonymous. This week, it was just more visible than usual.

The six-day rollback happened because the people most affected — professional creators, actors, people with specific legal exposure around likeness rights — recognized the risk immediately and said so loudly. Most AI defaults that quietly turn on don't have a SAG-AFTRA.

### TAKEAWAY

One thing to do right now.

Go into your Instagram settings. Use the search bar inside settings and type "AI." Look for anything related to data usage, content training, or AI. There's an opt-out toggle. Turn it off.

This takes about two minutes. It doesn't make your account private. It doesn't remove any content you've posted. It just tells Meta you're not consenting to your content being used as AI training or reference data.

If you already had it set to opt out before this week, you're fine. If you've never looked at that setting, now is the time.

The broader thing to hold onto: the protection here wasn't a platform privacy design that worked. It was a union that moved fast. Those are different things, and the difference matters for the next time this happens — which will be a different feature, from a different platform, without the Hollywood infrastructure around it.

Know that distinction going in.
