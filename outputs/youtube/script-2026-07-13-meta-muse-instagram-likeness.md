## HOOK — First 15 seconds

For four days last week, anyone on the internet could type your name into an AI tool and generate realistic photos of you.

Not cartoon versions. Realistic photos. Doing things you never did. In places you've never been.

Using photos from your public Instagram account. Without asking you. Without telling you.

This happened. It's over — for now. Here's what you actually need to know.

---

## SETUP — The problem, stated plainly

Meta launched a feature called Muse Image on July 8th. The marketing pitch was that it was a creative tool to generate AI photos for your social media pages.

The feature that wasn't in the marketing: anyone could @-mention *any* public Instagram account in a prompt. The AI would then pull from that account's public photos and generate new images of that person.

Their face. Their likeness. People who appeared in their photos — including children.

No notification to the person being generated. No consent required. If your account was public, you were included. Automatically. By default.

---

## EXPLANATION — What actually happened, in plain language

The feature worked like this: you'd open Meta AI, type something like "create a photo of @username at a 1970s concert," and the AI would use photos from that account to produce a realistic image of that person at a place they'd never been.

The results were not obviously AI-generated. They were realistic enough that — without context — you might not know they were fake.

SAG-AFTRA — the union that represents actors — moved within 48 hours. They put out an urgent alert, calling for an opt-in model. Not opt-out. Opt-in — meaning, you have to ask someone's permission before including them.

The actors' union doesn't usually issue urgent alerts about Instagram. This was different.

Meta pulled the @-mention feature from Instagram on July 10th — four days after launch.

But here's what actually matters: the feature is only paused on Instagram. Muse Image is still live on WhatsApp and the Meta AI app. The part that got pulled is the ability to @-mention Instagram accounts on Instagram specifically. The underlying AI model, and Meta's apparent belief that this was a reasonable thing to ship, is still there.

## NUANCE — The thing most explainer videos on this topic skip

Most of the coverage focused on the backlash and the reversal. That's accurate but it misses the more important story.

This is the fourth time in under two years that Meta has launched a product with the same design logic: assume consent, make it opt-out, and absorb the backlash.

Each time, they adjust. Each time, they try again. The business logic hasn't changed.

Here's the assumption baked into these products: having a public Instagram account means you've agreed to be included in whatever Meta builds next. That's not a legal standard — it's a design choice Meta keeps making. And they keep making it because, most of the time, most people don't push back hard enough to make it stop.

There's a specific detail in this story that I don't think got enough attention: public accounts on Instagram include, in Meta's definition, anyone with an account set to public — often because they want to be discoverable for clients, or run a small business, or want friends to find them without a follow request.

Those people did not sign up to have their face used as source material for AI image generation by strangers. But under the design logic Meta shipped, that's exactly what a public account meant — for 96 hours.

## TAKEAWAY — One clear thing to remember or do

One thing to walk away with: "public" and "opted-in" are not the same thing, even if Meta keeps treating them that way.

If you have a public Instagram account and you want to reduce your exposure: go to your Meta AI settings and look at what image generation features are enabled. If you haven't looked at those settings in the last week, it's worth checking — they may have changed.

And think carefully about the tradeoff of a public account. If you're a creative who needs discoverability, there's a real tension here. There's not a simple answer. But there's now a concrete reason to make the decision consciously rather than by default.

The feature was pulled. The question it raised — what does a company get to do with your public photos — wasn't.
