## SETUP

Adobe Acrobat — the software millions of people use to create, edit, annotate, and sign PDFs — has been automatically generating AI descriptions for images in documents.

The feature is presented as an accessibility improvement: it adds alt text to images that don't have any, so screen readers can describe images to visually impaired users. That's a real problem in the world of PDFs, and in theory, solving it matters.

The problem: Acrobat is also replacing alt text that humans already wrote. Intentional descriptions, added deliberately for accessibility, overwritten by AI-generated alternatives. Without a prompt. ## EXPLANATION

Alt text is the written description attached to an image in a digital document. Screen readers read it aloud to describe images to someone who can't see them. When a human writes it well — knowing what the image means, not just what it looks like — alt text can be specific and genuinely useful. When AI generates it, it produces a description based on what's visually in the image, with no understanding of context or intent.

Adobe's auto-generation feature is part of its accessibility tools. The intent is not bad. But overwriting existing human-written alt text is not an accessibility improvement. It's replacing intentional descriptions with guesses. The people most directly harmed by this are visually impaired users whose screen readers are now reading Adobe's AI output instead of the description a human deliberately wrote.

## NUANCE — the thing most coverage of this skips

Most coverage treats this as a settings problem: here's how to turn it off, here's where to find the checkbox.

The harder question is why it's on by default.

Turning a feature on by default means it reaches the maximum number of users immediately. Turning it off requires users to notice, find the setting, and take action — and most users don't do any of those things. Adobe knows this. "Default on" is not a neutral decision about user experience. It's a decision about how quickly a feature becomes normalized in users' workflows.

This is happening across creative software broadly. Photoshop has generative fill. Premiere has AI-assisted editing. InDesign has AI alt text. Acrobat has AI alt text. In each case, the AI feature is either on by default or prominently encouraged. The direction of travel is clear: your creative software is increasingly making decisions about your work in the background, on a schedule you didn't set, based on criteria you didn't choose.
