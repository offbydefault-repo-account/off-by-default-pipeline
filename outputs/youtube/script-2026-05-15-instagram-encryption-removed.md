---

### SETUP — The problem, stated plainly

Instagram has had end-to-end encrypted messaging since 2023. Three years. An optional feature, real and functional, that virtually no one used — because virtually no one knew it existed.

Meta announced in March 2026 that they were removing it. The encryption went away on May 8. Their stated reason: low adoption. Not enough people were using it.

That explanation sounds reasonable until you look at how the feature was designed — or more specifically, how it was hidden.

---

### EXPLANATION — What end-to-end encryption means, and what changed

**Plain language first:**
End-to-end encryption means that only you and the person you're messaging can read what you send. Not Instagram. Not Meta. Not their servers. If someone subpoenas Instagram for your DMs, Instagram hands over an unreadable scramble instead of your words. That's what encryption does.

Without it, the platform can read the content. Instagram DMs as of May 8 are readable by Meta. That access is used for content moderation — flagging messages that violate policies — and for ad targeting. Your conversations are part of the data infrastructure that tells Meta what to show you.

**How Instagram's encryption actually worked:**
When it existed, here's what using it required. You opened a specific conversation. You tapped a settings icon that wasn't labeled "privacy." You located the encryption option in a submenu. You enabled it. For that one conversation. Then you started a new conversation with someone else and did the same four steps again. If you forgot, that conversation was unencrypted. There was no global setting. No toggle. No prompt when you started a new message thread.

Meta sent no push notification that this feature existed. It appeared in no onboarding flow for new users. There was no Help Center article that surfaced it naturally. It was real, but it was invisible by design.

Then Meta measured how many people used it. The number was low. And they used that number to justify removing it.

*[Pause.]*

That argument is worth sitting with, because it sounds like evidence and isn't.

---

### NUANCE — What most explainer videos on this topic skip

*[Direct to camera.]*

Low adoption of a feature you deliberately buried does not prove that users didn't want privacy. It proves that defaults are everything.

Meta knows this. They've known it for years. WhatsApp — also owned by Meta — has end-to-end encryption on by default. Every message, every conversation, turned on automatically, no steps required. WhatsApp has more than two billion users. Their encrypted messaging has enormous adoption. You know why? Because it's the default.

Instagram made a different choice. They made encryption opt-in, per conversation, four steps deep, with no promotion. Then they looked at the resulting numbers and said: see, nobody wanted this.

That's not evidence of user preference. That's evidence of how powerful defaults are — and that Meta understood exactly what they were doing when they set those defaults.

There's one more thing worth naming, because most coverage skipped it. The Electronic Frontier Foundation called this "broken promises" — specifically because Instagram had, at various points, framed encryption as a commitment to user privacy. Removing a privacy feature you positioned as a value — and citing manufactured low numbers to justify it — is something a little different.

### TAKEAWAY — One clear thing to do or remember

*[Back to practical.]*

What this means for you right now: treat Instagram DMs the way you'd treat a text message or an email. Useful for scheduling and low-stakes back-and-forth. Not appropriate for anything genuinely sensitive — unreleased creative work, personal disclosures, conversations with sources, legal or medical matters.

If you want encrypted messaging and you're staying in the Meta ecosystem: WhatsApp. Its encryption is genuine, on by default, and audited. The metadata — who you're talking to, how often — is still visible to Meta, even if the content isn't. That's a real caveat. [YOUR TAKE ON THIS — is WhatsApp's Meta ownership a disqualifying factor? Give your actual read.]

If you want no Meta involvement at all: Signal. It's free, it's been audited by independent security researchers, and it has no advertising business model. The trade-off is that your contacts may not be on it.

For most people, the most actionable thing is the simplest: stop having sensitive conversations in Instagram DMs. You don't need a new app for that. You just need to know where you actually are.

*[Close.]*

The lesson here isn't specific to Instagram. Any time a platform tells you that a privacy feature had "low adoption," find out who controlled the defaults — and how long they'd been controlling them.
