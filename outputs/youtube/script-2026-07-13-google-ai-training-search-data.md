## SETUP — The problem, stated plainly

In June 2026, Google quietly updated its privacy settings. The change: photos, files, and audio recordings you upload to Google's search tools are now stored in a new category — and used to train its AI models.

By default. Without asking you first.

Most people never saw a pop-up. Most people never clicked "I agree." It just changed.

---

## EXPLANATION — What actually happened, in plain language

This is easier to understand with a specific example.

You're in your backyard and you find a plant you don't recognize. You open Google Lens, take a photo, and search. Google tells you what it is. You move on.

Under Google's June 2026 update, that photo is now saved in your Search Services History — a new category of data — and used to train Google's AI models. Unless you've turned it off.

Same with voice searches. If you've ever held up your phone and spoken your search out loud, that audio recording may now be in this training pool.

Same with files. If you've ever uploaded a document to ask Google an AI question, that document may be in there.

The update covers Google Search, Google Lens, Google Maps, Google Translate, Google Shopping, Google News, and Google's AI Mode — basically every Google product most people use every day.

The opt-out is at myactivity.google.com. Go there, sign in, and find the Search Services History tab. You have two options: turn off the whole thing, or just uncheck the "Save Media" box inside it to stop image and audio collection while keeping your text search history.

The opt-out takes about 90 seconds once you find the page. The hard part is knowing to look.

One important thing: turning it off doesn't delete what Google has already collected. To clear that, you'll need to hit the delete option separately — same page, different button. ## NUANCE — The thing most explainers on this topic skip

Here's the part most coverage missed.

When Google announced this, they framed it as giving users "more control." And in a narrow sense, that's true — they created a new setting.

But "more control" in this context means: we started doing something new, and we're giving you a way to stop it after the fact.

That framing deserves scrutiny.

Most people's mental model of their Google data is: my search history is being collected, I've accepted that, it's text in a box, it's not my most sensitive information.

The expansion to images, audio recordings, and uploaded files is a different category of data. A photo of your medication. Your voice recorded in your living room. A contract or health document you uploaded to ask an AI question about.

This isn't more of the same thing. It's a different thing — richer, more contextual, more personal. And it got folded into the same opt-out framework as your search history, quietly, in June.

There's also a technical detail worth knowing: your Web & App Activity setting and your Search Services History setting are separate. Changing one doesn't affect the other. If you've ever gone through and turned things off in your Google account before, that didn't cover this new category. You need to check it specifically. ## TAKEAWAY — One clear thing to remember or do

One thing to walk away with:

Privacy settings don't stay set. Companies update what they collect, and the settings don't always send you a notification when they do.

Search Services History is a new setting for new behavior that started in June 2026. It's worth 90 seconds to go turn it off.

Not because Google is uniquely bad. Not because you have something to hide. But because it's your photos and your voice, and whether a company uses them to build AI products is a decision you're allowed to make for yourself.

The link to myactivity.google.com is in the description. The tab you want is Search Services History.
