## LINKEDIN

### Newsletter
A UK writer won a short story competition this month. Her entry was run through an AI detection tool by the organizers. It came back 100 percent machine-generated. She wrote every word.

This is not an outlier. Publishers have canceled contracts with human authors after similar scores. Universities have opened misconduct proceedings against students who didn't use AI at all. Five federal lawsuits from falsely flagged students are working through the courts.

The instinct when you hear this is to assume it's a calibration problem — that the tool just needs better training. Research published this week says that instinct is wrong.

AI text detectors look for statistical patterns: controlled vocabulary, consistent sentence structure, logical transitions, predictable word choices. Those patterns appear in AI writing. They also appear in skilled human writing. Spend years refining your craft — trimming, finding your voice, tightening your sentences — and your prose becomes statistically indistinguishable from well-trained AI output. Not because you used AI. Because you got good.

The research, from Pindrop and the Authors Guild, showed using formal probability theory that any detector with real detection power will necessarily flag skilled human writers. Not occasionally. Necessarily. As a mathematical requirement. Better algorithms don't fix this. More training data doesn't fix this. The false positive problem is structural.

One finding worth sitting with: the false positive rate is not evenly distributed. Researchers documented a 61 percent false positive rate for essays written by Chinese students in English, versus 5 percent for native speakers on the same tests. The tool most aggressively penalizes writers who've worked hardest to express themselves precisely in a second language.

If you write anything that anyone evaluates: document your process now. Keep your drafts. Use version history. If your work is ever challenged, your defense will not be a competing score from a different tool. It will be your process, your drafts, your timestamps.

### YouTube
The instinct when you hear about AI detectors falsely flagging human writers is to assume someone just needs to train the tool better.

New research published this week says that instinct is wrong — and understanding why matters more than knowing what any particular tool scored you.

These detectors work by identifying statistical patterns. AI writing has recognizable tendencies: consistent structure, controlled vocabulary, logical transitions. The tools learn to spot those patterns and return a probability estimate.

The problem: skilled human writers produce the same patterns. Spend years developing your craft — cutting clutter, finding your voice, tightening your sentences — and your writing looks, statistically, a lot like well-trained AI output. Not because you used AI. Because you got good at writing.

Research from Pindrop and the Authors Guild, published this week, showed using formal probability theory that any detector with meaningful detection power will necessarily generate false positives among skilled writers whose prose overlaps statistically with AI output. Not occasionally — necessarily. As a mathematical requirement. The thing these tools are looking for doesn't belong exclusively to AI, so you cannot build a system that reliably catches one without also catching the other.

The numbers: NeurIPS — one of the world's most prestigious AI research conferences — rejected 178 submitted papers after a detection tool scored them 100 percent AI-generated. AI researchers. The people who build this technology. Flagged by tools their own field created.

And the false positive burden isn't evenly distributed. Researchers found a 61 percent false positive rate for essays by non-native English speakers writing carefully in English — versus 5 percent for native speakers. Precision in your second language is penalized.

If you're in a position to decide whether these tools get used — teacher, editor, publisher, competition organizer — the research is now explicit enough that deploying AI detection for high-stakes decisions is indefensible. That's already documented in federal court records.
