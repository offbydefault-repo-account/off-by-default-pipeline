## LINKEDIN

**Variant A:**

"Publicly available" and "consented to" are not the same phrase.

That distinction is at the center of Amnesty International's new report, "Unlawful by Design," and it's the part most coverage has quietly glossed over.

The report isn't another call for AI regulation. It's a detailed legal brief arguing that the training data pipelines behind ChatGPT, Google's Gemini, Meta's Llama, Midjourney, and Stable Diffusion are structurally incompatible with international human rights law. Specifically: mass-scraping billions of people's posts, photos, and publicly visible writing — without individual knowledge or consent — is a privacy violation at scale. The fact that the content was technically accessible doesn't change that.

The title "Unlawful by Design" is deliberate. It isn't saying a mistake was made or that corners were cut. It's saying the architecture required the violation. These systems couldn't have been built at this scale any other way. The mass collection wasn't a side effect — it was the prerequisite.

There's a version of this most people have already dismissed: "I posted things publicly. What did I expect?" But scale changes the nature of the act. There's a real difference between "people can read your blog" and "a company automatically harvests your blog, along with hundreds of millions of others, to train commercial products they'll sell back to you at a subscription fee."

The report also raises something most coverage skips: when people figure out that what they write publicly may be scraped to train AI systems they'll later interact with, it changes what they're willing to say. Amnesty frames that chilling effect on expression as a specific, measurable human rights harm — not a philosophical concern.

The data that was scraped is already in the training sets. You can't retrieve it. What you can do is understand what these tools were built on — and make your choices from that position.
