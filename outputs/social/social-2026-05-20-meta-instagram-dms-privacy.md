## LINKEDIN

> If you've ever sent something through Instagram DMs that you considered private — a client conversation, health information, something financial — there's something worth knowing.
>
> On May 8th, Meta quietly removed Instagram's optional end-to-end encryption for direct messages. There was no push notification. No in-app banner. The announcement appeared in support documentation, which is where companies put things when they'd prefer you didn't notice.
>
> Instagram DMs were never fully private by default. Meta has always been able to read the standard message experience. But the end-to-end encrypted option was meaningfully different — when it was on, your messages scrambled before they left your device and only unscrambled on the recipient's. Meta's servers relayed the message but couldn't read the contents.
>
> That option is gone now.
>
> Most coverage on this correctly notes that Instagram DMs were "never really private anyway." That's true. But it misses what actually happened: the one mechanism that made them genuinely private in a technical sense was removed without warning from users who had specifically sought it out.
>
> Instagram uses the word "private" a lot — private accounts, close friends, restricted profiles. Those features control who can see your public content. None of them make the platform itself unable to read your messages. End-to-end encryption was the one thing that did.
>
> It's worth understanding what "private" actually means on any platform you're using for real conversations.
