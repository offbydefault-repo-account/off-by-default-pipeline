## LINKEDIN

On May 8, 2026, Meta removed the option to make your Instagram DMs private — even from Meta.

Most people didn't notice because most people didn't know the option existed. Instagram offered end-to-end encryption as an opt-in feature in Direct Messages. If you had it turned on, your conversations were protected even from Meta's servers — only you and the person you were talking to could read them.

If you hadn't turned it on — which was most users, because it was buried in settings and never surfaced clearly — you got no notice when it disappeared. Because from Meta's perspective, nothing had changed for you.

Meta's explanation: "Very few people were opting in, so we're removing this option."

That reasoning is worth reading carefully. Low adoption of a privacy feature — one that was never prominently promoted — is being used as justification for eliminating it entirely. Not improving it. Not surfacing it. Removing it.

In 2019, Mark Zuckerberg published a widely-cited post describing a "privacy-focused social networking" vision and specifically named end-to-end encryption for Instagram DMs as a goal. That post was referenced for years as evidence that Meta took privacy seriously. The feature existed — minimally, quietly — for several years. On May 8, it didn't anymore.

WhatsApp, also owned by Meta, still has end-to-end encryption on by default. Signal is free and takes five minutes to set up.

This week's newsletter covers what actually changed, what Meta can technically access now, and the quickest way to move private conversations somewhere they stay private.
