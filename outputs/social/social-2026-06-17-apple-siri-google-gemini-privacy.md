## LINKEDIN (optimal ~800 characters)

Apple spent years making privacy a brand promise. Craig Federighi has said "privacy in AI is nonnegotiable" at two consecutive keynotes. At WWDC last week, he said it again — right before announcing that the most capable version of Siri ever built hands off your most complex questions to Google Gemini, running on Google Cloud servers.

That's not a contradiction in bad faith. It's a tradeoff — and tradeoffs are worth understanding clearly before they land on your phone.

Here's how the new Siri architecture actually works: simple requests (timers, weather, music) stay on your iPhone. Moderate requests go to Apple's own Private Cloud Compute. Complex requests — the ones that need real reasoning, multi-step tasks, cross-app context — route to Google's infrastructure.

Apple says Google doesn't retain those queries. There's no independent audit of Google's end of that arrangement yet.

What this means for you depends on who you already are online. If you use Gmail, Google Maps, and Google Search, the change is marginal — you're already deep in Google's data ecosystem. If you chose iPhone specifically to stay out of it, this update changes that calculation.

iOS 27 ships this fall. You have time to understand what you're opting into before it arrives.
