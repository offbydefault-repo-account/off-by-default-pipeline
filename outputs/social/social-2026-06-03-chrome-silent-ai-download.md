## LINKEDIN

**Variant A:**

There's a 4GB file on your computer that you didn't put there.

It's called `weights.bin`. It lives inside a folder in your Chrome profile directory called `OptGuideOnDeviceModel`. Google installed it — silently, automatically, without a notification or permission prompt — as part of a rollout of Gemini Nano, the smallest model in their Gemini AI family.

If you found it and deleted it, Chrome put it back.

The features it runs aren't useless: an AI writing assistant, on-device scam detection, and something called the Summarizer API — which third-party websites can call directly, using your device's processing power to summarize content on their behalf. Your hardware, their feature.

Here's the part that complicates the obvious reaction: on-device AI genuinely is more private than the cloud alternative. When the model runs locally, your requests don't travel to Google's servers. Apple has been making exactly this case about its own AI features for years — and they're not wrong.

So the engineering choice was defensible. The consent process wasn't.

"Better than the cloud alternative" is not the same as "you agreed to this." You get to make decisions about your own hardware. Google made this one for you.

The good news: Chrome added a setting. Go to Settings → Privacy and Security → AI features. If you see a toggle for on-device AI, you can turn it off and remove the model. Not everyone has this setting yet — the rollout has been uneven. If yours isn't there, your options are switching browsers or waiting.

Firefox and Brave handle AI features differently if you want a browser that asks before it installs. The full walkthrough — including how to check if the model is on your device right now — is in this week's issue.
