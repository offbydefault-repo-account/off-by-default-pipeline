## LINKEDIN

There's a 4-gigabyte file sitting on most people's computers right now that they never downloaded.

Google added it through a Chrome update — no notification, no consent prompt, no pop-up. The file is called `weights.bin` and it's a compressed version of Google's Gemini Nano AI model. Chrome installed it as part of its own infrastructure, not as a feature you could choose.

If you found it and deleted it before knowing what it was, Chrome re-downloaded it the next time you opened the browser.

Google's argument is that this is actually good for your privacy: the AI runs locally, on your hardware, not Google's servers. That argument has real merit. Local processing means your keystrokes don't end up in a cloud log somewhere.

But the argument lands differently when it comes paired with an installation you weren't informed about.

This isn't a surveillance story. The model runs on your device, not Google's. It's a consent story — about who decides what gets written to your hard drive, and when.

In any other context, software that installs itself silently, repels deletion, and reinstates itself after removal would get flagged as a security risk. When a browser maker does it with their own AI model, it's called a rollout.

The feature may be useful. The scam detection it enables is genuinely helpful. But Google could have asked — and if they had, a lot of people would have said yes.

An opt-out was quietly added in February. It exists and it works. If you'd like to check whether you have the model and remove it if you choose, the full steps are in this week's Off by Default newsletter.
