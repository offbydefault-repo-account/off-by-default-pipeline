### LinkedIn (~800 characters, max 1,800)

In early July, Meta launched something called Muse Image. If you had a public Instagram account, you were already opted in before most people had heard the name.

The feature let any Instagram user type your username into a prompt and generate new AI images using your photos as reference material. Your face, your aesthetic — as raw material for anyone with access to the tool.

SAG-AFTRA issued a statement three days after launch. CAA called for removal. Meta pulled the Instagram integration six days in.

Here's the part that got less coverage: they didn't remove Muse Image. They removed the feature that let users tag Instagram accounts. The broader tool still runs in the Meta AI app and WhatsApp. The opt-out is still there, which means the default is still opt-in.

If you have a public Instagram account, go into your settings, search for "AI," and turn off whatever data-usage toggle you find. It takes two minutes. It doesn't make your account private. It just removes your consent from a decision that was made for you.

### LinkedIn (~800 characters, max 1,800)

Six days.

That's how long Meta's Muse Image ran on Instagram before they pulled it. And the reason it was pulled in six days — not six months, not after a congressional hearing — is worth understanding.

It moved fast because the people most affected saw it immediately: professional creators, actors, people who've spent years thinking about likeness rights. They said so loudly. The cost of keeping it became higher than the cost of pulling it.

That's a model. Not a guaranteed outcome — it required a union with enough leverage. Most AI defaults that quietly turn on don't have a SAG-AFTRA.

The accessible vs. reusable distinction is the thing worth carrying out of this story. A photo posted for your followers is not automatically a photo you offered as reference material for AI image generation by strangers. Platforms have been treating those as the same thing. This week, it was just more visible than usual.
