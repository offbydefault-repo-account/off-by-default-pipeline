## LINKEDIN

### Newsletter
Adobe Acrobat has been making changes to PDF files that their owners didn't request and don't know about.

A game publisher called Evil Hat Productions updated the PDFs they sell for their tabletop roleplaying games. When players downloaded the new version, they found descriptive text in the files that Evil Hat hadn't written. Adobe's AI had generated it automatically during a save operation — and in some cases, it hadn't just added text where there was none. It replaced text that was already there.

The text in question is alt text: written descriptions attached to images that screen readers use to describe images to people who are visually impaired. When a human writes alt text well — knowing what the image means in context, not just what it looks like — it makes a document genuinely accessible. Adobe replaced it with AI-generated alternatives. No warning. No opt-in. No checkbox.

Adobe frames this as an accessibility improvement. Generating alt text for images that genuinely have none is useful — a lot of PDFs in the world are missing it entirely, and that's a real problem. But overwriting descriptions someone already wrote, for a specific purpose, is not an improvement. It's the opposite.

If you create PDFs professionally: open your documents, go to the Accessibility panel in Acrobat, and look at the descriptions on your images. If they seem oddly generic or literal, they may have been replaced.

The setting can be disabled in Edit > Preferences > Accessibility — exact path varies by version.

The harder question isn't how to turn it off. It's why it shipped on by default — and what else your software is doing to your files in the background that you haven't checked yet.

### YouTube
Adobe is making changes to PDF files that their owners didn't authorize and may not have noticed.

The specific feature: Acrobat now auto-generates AI alt text — written image descriptions — during save operations. Alt text matters because screen readers use it to describe images to visually impaired users. Written by a person who understands the image in context, it's genuinely useful. Generated by AI based on visual appearance alone, it's a guess.

The part that's harder to dismiss: Acrobat isn't only adding descriptions to images that had none. It's replacing descriptions that humans had already written. Intentional, specific alt text — overwritten by AI output, without a prompt and without a warning.

This happened to Evil Hat Productions when they updated their game PDFs. Players noticed text that the publisher hadn't put there. Evil Hat hadn't written it.

If you use Adobe Acrobat for documents, portfolios, educational materials, or anything with images: check your alt text. The Accessibility panel will show you what's attached to each image. If the descriptions seem oddly literal or generic for what you put in the file, they may have been replaced.

This is also a pricing story. Adobe raised monthly rates in 2025 and bundled Firefly AI credits into plans at higher cost. Your creative software now costs more and is making decisions about your files in the background, on a schedule you didn't set. Whether that's a reasonable trade depends on what you're making and what those decisions are quietly replacing.
