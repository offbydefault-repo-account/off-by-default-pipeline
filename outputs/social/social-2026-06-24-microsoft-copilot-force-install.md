## LINKEDIN

### Newsletter variant

There's a detail buried in the Microsoft Copilot rollout story that most coverage is skipping.

Microsoft has now attempted to auto-install Copilot on Windows machines three times. The first two attempts were walked back after backlash from IT administrators and users. This third attempt — running June 15 through July 15 — is proceeding.

Here's the part worth sitting with: users in the European Economic Area aren't getting this automatic install. Not because of a technical limitation. Because EU regulations require different defaults.

That gap tells you something precise. When companies say "this is just how the product works," the EEA exemption shows there's always another option — one that involves asking permission first. The choice not to ask US users isn't a constraint. It's a product decision.

The Copilot features themselves aren't the problem. AI that summarizes your emails or helps you draft a document is genuinely useful if you choose it. The issue is what it means when "it's already on your machine" becomes the default — and the work falls to you to remove it if you didn't want it.

That's a small shift in who holds the friction. It tends to matter more than most product launches do.
