## LINKEDIN

### Newsletter variant

There's one comparison that cuts through most of the debate about Gmail's Gemini AI rollout.

If you use Gmail in the United States, Google's AI features — email summaries, suggested replies, inbox interaction data — were turned on by default. You didn't have to do anything to enable them.

If you use Gmail in the EU, UK, or Japan, those same features are off by default. You have to turn them on if you want them.

Same company. Same product. Different defaults — because different laws.

This isn't about whether the features are useful (some are). It's about what the default communicates. When a company has the technical capability to ask first and doesn't, it's telling you something about how it weighs your preferences against its product goals.

There's also a class-action lawsuit moving forward that makes exactly this argument: the claim is that Google didn't give US users meaningful notice or real control before enabling these features. The EU disparity is central to the case.

If you want to change this: it takes three separate settings locations in Gmail to fully opt out. And doing so also disables features like Smart Reply that have nothing to do with AI training — Google bundled them together in a way that makes opting out more expensive than it needs to be.
