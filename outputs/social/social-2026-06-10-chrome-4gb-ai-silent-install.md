## LINKEDIN

There's a 4GB file on most Chrome users' computers. It's called weights.bin. Chrome installed it — silently, in the background, with no notification — as part of a rollout that's been running since 2025.

When a security researcher found it in May 2026 and published the details, the story spread fast. Users reported it had been happening for nearly a year with no one being told.

The file contains Gemini Nano — Google's on-device AI model. The intended use is local tasks like writing assist: your input stays on your device, no server round-trip. There's a defensible reason for it to exist. There isn't a good reason it arrived without a conversation.

Here's what most coverage got wrong: the local Gemini Nano model is actually the less concerning part of what's happening.

Chrome has a visible AI Mode button in the address bar. Knowing about the local model, the natural assumption is that button uses it — that AI searches stay on your device. That assumption is wrong. AI Mode routes your queries to Google's cloud servers. The 4GB local model handles background features. The prominent AI feature in your browser is the one sending data to Google.

Both things are true at once: the hidden file is a genuine storage concern. And the more consequential question is the AI feature you can actually see.
